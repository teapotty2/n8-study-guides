NCLEX Takeaways

Overview & Legal Foundations

Emergency nursing requires specialized training in crisis assessment, prioritization, and continuous monitoring. The ED functions as a temporary point on the continuum of care where patients are rapidly assessed, treated, and referred for ongoing care.

Key Insight: An emergency is whatever the patient or the family considers it to be. The ED is used for both urgent and nonurgent problems.

EMTALA (Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act)

  • All providers must screen patients if they present with an emergency or are in labor
  • Patient must be stable before transfer to another facility
  • Must obtain patient consent for transfer, acceptance by receiving facility, and send documentation

Safety & Sentinel Events

Warning: The most common sentinel events in the ED are delays to care and medication errors. Safety is always the FIRST priority.

Occupational Health Risks

ED nurses are at increased risk for communicable disease exposure. Must adhere to standard precautions and transmission-based precautions for patients with potentially infectious conditions.

Family-Centered Care

  • Allow family to stay with patient when possible to alleviate anxiety
  • Treat unconscious patients as if conscious (touch, call by name, explain procedures)
  • Do NOT give sedation to grieving family members (masks/delays grieving process)
  • Encourage family to view the body if they wish; cover disfigured areas first
Pause & Think: Why shouldn't you sedate grieving family members?

Sedation can mask or delay the grieving process, which is necessary to achieve emotional equilibrium and prevent prolonged depression. The family needs to experience and process their grief to heal.

Triage Systems

Triage (from French trier, "to sort") sorts patients by severity of health problems and immediacy of treatment needs.

Emergency Severity Index (ESI) — 5 Levels

LevelCategoryReassessmentDescription
1ResuscitationContinuousLife-threatening, requires immediate intervention
2EmergentEvery 15 minHigh risk; confused, lethargic, disoriented; severe pain
3UrgentEvery 30 minSerious but not immediately life-threatening
4Less UrgentEvery 60 minRequires 1 resource; not urgent
5NonurgentEvery 120 minRequires no resources; minor complaint
NCLEX Alert: Know the critical difference: ED triage = most critically ill get most resources, regardless of outcome. Disaster triage = greatest good for the greatest number, based on survival likelihood and resource consumption. This is a common test question!

Triage Data Collection

The triage nurse collects baseline data including: full vital signs with pain assessment, history of current event, past medical history, neurologic assessment, weight, allergies (especially latex and medications), intimate partner violence screening, last tetanus immunization, last menstrual period, and last meal eaten.

Pause & Think: Why is "last meal eaten" asked during triage?

This information is critical if general anesthesia may be needed (risk of aspiration) or if the patient is unconscious. A full stomach increases the risk of vomiting and aspiration during intubation or surgery.

Primary Survey (ABCDE)

The primary survey focuses on stabilizing life-threatening conditions. It follows the ABCDE mnemonic:

AAirway — Establish a patent airway. Protect the cervical spine in all trauma patients.
BBreathing — Provide adequate ventilation. Assess bilateral breath sounds. In trauma: C-spine protection + chest injury assessment immediately after airway.
CCirculation — Evaluate and restore cardiac output. Control hemorrhage, prevent/treat shock, restore effective circulation. Prevent hypothermia. Check peripheral pulses.
DDisability — Assess neurologic status using Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) and AVPU mnemonic.
EExposure — Undress the patient to identify all wounds and injuries (may require cutting clothing).

AVPU Mnemonic (Quick Neuro Assessment)

A — Alert? → V — Responds to Verbal? → P — Responds to Pain? → U — Unresponsive?
Clinical Pearl: A tension pneumothorax can mimic hypovolemia. Always complete the ventilatory assessment before hemorrhage assessment in the primary survey.

Secondary Survey

After the primary survey stabilizes life-threatening conditions, the secondary survey provides a comprehensive head-to-toe assessment:

Head-to-Toe Assessment

Diagnostics & Labs

Monitoring Devices

Airway Management

Foreign Body Obstruction

Complete Obstruction
Cannot speak, breathe, or cough. Universal distress signal (clutching neck). Immediate intervention needed.
Partial Obstruction
Can cough and breathe spontaneously. Encourage forceful coughing. Monitor closely.
Warning: A weak cough + high-pitched noise on inhalation = treat as complete obstruction. Cyanosis and loss of consciousness are late signs — act before these develop!

Airway Devices (Escalation Ladder)

Head-tilt/Chin-lift or Jaw Thrust (protect C-spine)
↓
OPA (Oropharyngeal Airway) — unconscious; measure lip to ear; prevents tongue obstruction
↓
NPA (Nasopharyngeal Airway) — through nares; do NOT use with facial trauma/basal skull fracture
↓
ETT (Endotracheal Tube) — for patients needing more ventilatory support
↓

↓

Key Insight: Cricothyroidotomy is reserved for emergencies when ETT is contraindicated: maxillofacial trauma, C-spine injuries, laryngospasm, laryngeal edema, hemorrhage into neck tissue, or obstruction of the larynx.

OPA Insertion Technique

  1. Measure from lip to ear
  2. Extend head (only if C-spine uninjured)
  3. Open mouth and insert with tip facing up toward roof of mouth
  4. Rotate 180° so tip points down toward pharynx
  5. Ensure tongue is not pushed into airway
Pause & Think: Why is a nasopharyngeal airway contraindicated with basal skull fracture?

With a basal skull fracture, the bones at the base of the skull are disrupted. The NPA could enter the brain cavity instead of the pharynx, causing devastating neurological injury. Always use an oropharyngeal airway instead in suspected facial trauma or basal skull fracture.

Hemorrhage

Assessment

Cool, moist skin → ↑ HR → ↓ BP → ↓ Urine output → Delayed cap refill → SHOCK
Clinical Pearl: Internal hemorrhage can hide in the retroperitoneum, pelvis, chest, and thighs — producing shock without external evidence of bleeding. A patient with ↑HR, ↓BP, thirst, cool/moist skin, and NO visible bleeding should be evaluated for internal hemorrhage.

Management

Fluid Replacement

  • Two large-bore IVs (preferably in uninjured extremity)
  • Fluids: LR, NS, albumin
  • Blood products: PRBCs for massive hemorrhage, + platelets, cryo, FFP
Warning: Large-volume blood transfusion should use a warmer when possible. Refrigerated blood has a core cooling effect that can lead to cardiac arrest and coagulopathy.

External Bleeding Control

1Rapid assessment — apply firm, direct pressure
2Pressure dressing + elevate injured part
3Tourniquet if bleeding cannot be stopped with pressure (apply proximal to wound)
NCLEX Alert: Tourniquet management: Tag patient with "T" on forehead noting location and time. Document time of application and removal. Keep tourniquet on until patient is in OR.
Pause & Think: A patient has tachycardia, falling BP, and cool skin but no visible bleeding. What should you suspect and what nursing actions are priority?

Suspect internal hemorrhage. Priority actions: maintain supine position, establish two large-bore IVs, administer PRBCs/plasma/platelets at rapid rate, monitor continuously, prepare for possible surgery or ICU transfer. Do NOT wait for external signs — internal bleeding in the retroperitoneum, pelvis, chest, or thighs can be massive.

Key Insight: In suspected criminal activity: Do NOT cut through tears, holes, blood stains, or dirt on clothing. Multiple Trauma

Definition: Single catastrophic event causing life-threatening injuries to ≥2 distinct organs or organ systems.

The Deadly Triad

Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy = Deadly Triad

Priority Management

1Establish airway and ventilation
2Control hemorrhage
3Prevent and treat hypovolemic shock
4Assess for head and neck injuries
5Evaluate other injuries — expose and reassess head, neck, chest; assess abdomen, back, extremities
6Splint fractures → reassess pulses and neurovascular status
7More thorough ongoing examination; diagnostic studies
Common Mistake: Focusing on the most visually dramatic injury. The least significant-appearing injury may be the most lethal. A pelvic fracture (not visible) can cause massive hemorrhage, while an obvious arm amputation may have already stopped bleeding via vasoconstriction.
NCLEX Alert: Always assume cervical spine injury in trauma patients until ruled out. Remove backboard early to prevent skin breakdown. Preferred IV crystalloid is LR (NS can worsen metabolic acidosis).

Intra-Abdominal Injuries

Penetrating Trauma
  • GSW, stab wounds
  • Usually require surgery
  • High incidence of hollow organ injury (small bowel)
  • Liver = most frequently injured solid organ
Blunt Trauma
  • MVC, falls, blows, explosions
  • Harder to detect
  • Delayed complications more common
  • Associated with chest, head, extremity injuries

Assessment Findings

  • Absent bowel sounds = possible intraperitoneal involvement
  • Progressive distention, guarding, tenderness, rigidity, rebound tenderness
  • Referred pain: Left shoulder = spleen bleeding; Right shoulder = liver laceration
  • Diagnostics: FAST ultrasound, CT, diagnostic peritoneal lavage (DPL)

Key Management

  • If viscera protruding → cover with sterile, moist saline dressings
  • Hold oral fluids (anticipate surgery)
  • NG tube to aspirate stomach contents
  • Tetanus and antibiotic prophylaxis

Crush Injuries & Rhabdomyolysis

Crush injuries occur when a patient is caught between opposing forces (run over, trapped under, crushed between objects).

↓

↓

  1. Myalgias (muscle cramps)
  2. Generalized muscle weakness
  3. Darkened urine

Serum lactic acid <2.5 mmol/L = indication of successful resuscitation. Monitor for compartment syndrome → fasciotomy if neurovascular function compromised.

Heat Stroke

Heat stroke is an acute medical emergency caused by failure of the heat-regulating mechanisms. It is the inability to maintain cardiac output in the face of high body temperatures.

Nonexertional

Prolonged exposure to environmental temp >39.2°C (102.5°F). Common during extended heat waves with high humidity.

Exertional

Strenuous activity in hot environment. Occurs in healthy individuals during sports/work. Hyperthermia from inadequate heat loss.

Manifestations

CNS dysfunction → Temp ≥40.6°C (105°F) → Hot, dry skin → Anhidrosis → Tachypnea + Hypotension + Tachycardia

Management

Warning: Reduce body temperature to 39°C (102°F) within 1 hour. Mortality is directly related to duration of hyperthermia. Stop cooling at 38°C (100.4°F) to prevent iatrogenic hypothermia.

Cooling Methods

  • Cool sheets, towels, or sponging with cool water
  • Ice to neck, groin, chest, and axillae
  • Cooling blankets
  • Cold water bath immersion (optimal method)

Additional Interventions

  • 100% oxygen (hypermetabolic state)
  • IV NS or LR (give carefully — myocardial injury risk from high temp)
  • Core temp monitoring: rectal, bladder, or esophageal thermistor
  • Meds: anticonvulsants (seizures), benzodiazepines (diazepam), potassium, sodium bicarbonate, chlorpromazine (shivering)
  • Monitor for: DIC, ATN from rhabdomyolysis, permanent liver/cardiac/CNS damage
Clinical Pearl — Older Adults: Circulatory systems cannot compensate for heat stress. Less subcutaneous tissue, decreased thirst (often from fear of incontinence) → inadequate hydration. Fans alone are NOT adequate; direct patients to air-conditioned locations.
Pause & Think: Why do you stop cooling at 38°C rather than normal body temp?

The body continues to cool even after active cooling measures stop. If you cool to 37°C, the patient's temperature will continue dropping below normal, causing iatrogenic hypothermia. Hyperthermia may also recur spontaneously within 3-4 hours, so continuous monitoring is essential.

Frostbite

Frostbite is trauma from exposure to freezing temperatures causing freezing of intracellular and intercellular fluid, resulting in cellular and vascular damage.

Manifestations

Hard, cold, insensitive to touch. May appear white or mottled blue-white. May turn red and painful as rewarmed. Extent of injury is not always initially known.

Management

  • Controlled rapid rewarming: 37°-40°C (98.6°-104°F) circulating bath for 30-40 min intervals
  • Analgesics for pain (rewarming is very painful)
  • Do NOT massage or handle the affected area
  • If feet involved, do NOT walk
  • After rewarming: elevate, sterile gauze between digits, bulky dressing
  • Hemorrhagic blebs: Leave intact. Nonhemorrhagic blisters: Débride
  • Monitor for hyperkalemia (release of K+ from damaged cells) and hypovolemia
  • Discharge: avoid tobacco, alcohol, caffeine (vasoconstrictive effects)
NCLEX Alert: Hemorrhagic blebs = leave intact (disruption causes further tissue damage). Nonhemorrhagic blisters = débride. Monitor for hyperkalemia (K+ released from damaged cells) and hypovolemia. Do NOT rub or massage frostbitten tissue — ice crystals can cause further cellular damage.

Hypothermia

Core temperature ≤35°C (95°F). Leads to physiologic changes in all organ systems.

Key Insight: Hypothermia takes precedence over frostbite treatment. Alcohol increases susceptibility by causing systemic vasodilation. High-risk groups: older adults, infants, concurrent illness, homeless, trauma victims.

Assessment Findings

Progressive deterioration: apathy → poor judgment → ataxia → drowsiness → pulmonary edema → coagulopathy → coma. Shivering suppressed at <32.2°C (90°F). Cardiac arrhythmias may occur. Peripheral pulses may be undetectable.

Rewarming Methods

MethodSeverityTemp RangeTechniques
Active core (internal)Moderate-severe<28°-32.2°CCardiopulmonary bypass, warm fluid admin, warm humidified O2, warm peritoneal lavage
Passive/active externalMild32.2°-35°CWarm blankets, over-bed heaters, forced-air warming blankets
Warning — Core Temperature Afterdrop: Cold blood returning from extremities has high lactic acid levels. When it returns to the core, it causes a further drop in core temperature and can trigger cardiac arrhythmias and electrolyte disturbances. Monitor for V-fib as temp rises from 31°-32°C.
NCLEX Alert: Defibrillation is ineffective at <31°C (88°F) — the patient must be rewarmed first. A hypothermic patient who appears dead should not be pronounced until they are warm and dead.
Pause & Think: Why is defibrillation ineffective in severe hypothermia?

At temperatures below 31°C, the myocardium is too cold to respond to electrical defibrillation. The cold-induced myocardial irritability makes the heart resistant to electrical conversion. The patient must first be rewarmed to at least 31°C before defibrillation can be effective. Even moving or touching a severely hypothermic patient can trigger spontaneous ventricular fibrillation.

Treatment Goals

2Supportive care for vital organ function
3Administer specific antidote
4Hasten elimination of absorbed poison

Food Poisoning

Botulism and fish poisoning can cause respiratory paralysis and death. Key assessment: severe vomiting → alkalosis; severe diarrhea → acidosis. Note that diarrhea is usually absent with botulism.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

CO Inhalation
Carbon monoxide enters lungs
↓
Binds to Hemoglobin (200x more readily than O2)
Forms carboxyhemoglobin — CANNOT transport oxygen
↓
Tissue Hypoxia
CNS symptoms predominate: headache, weakness, dizziness, confusion → coma
NCLEX Alert: Two critical facts: (1) Skin color is NOT a reliable sign of CO poisoning. (2) Pulse oximetry is NOT valid — it reads carboxyhemoglobin as oxygen-saturated hemoglobin, giving a falsely high reading. The patient may appear intoxicated from cerebral hypoxia.

Management

  • Move to fresh air immediately
  • CPR as necessary
  • 100% oxygen (or hyperbaric oxygen) until carboxyhemoglobin <5%
  • Keep quiet, prevent chilling
  • Do NOT allow smoking or alcohol
  • Monitor for permanent brain damage: psychoses, spastic paralysis, ataxia, visual disturbances
  • If intentional → psychiatric consultation
Pause & Think: Why does pulse oximetry give falsely normal readings in CO poisoning?

Pulse oximetry measures hemoglobin saturation but cannot distinguish between oxyhemoglobin and carboxyhemoglobin. Since CO is bound to the hemoglobin molecule, the device detects the hemoglobin as "saturated" and reports a normal or high SpO2, even though the hemoglobin is carrying CO instead of oxygen. You must use co-oximetry (arterial blood gas) to measure carboxyhemoglobin levels directly.

Substance Abuse

Acetaminophen — #1 Overdosed Drug

Toxic level: ≥140 mg/kg body weight.

StageTimingManifestations
IWithin 24hGI upset, diaphoresis, possible metabolic acidosis and coma if severe
II24-48hMonitor liver function and coagulation studies
IIIAfter 48hHepatic encephalopathy, jaundice, vomiting, RUQ pain, coagulopathy, hypoglycemia, AKI
Quick Reference: Antidote = N-acetylcysteine (NAC). Replenishes liver enzymes. 18 doses every 4 hours. Charcoal absorbs NAC — do NOT administer together. Repeat NAC dose if patient vomits.

Opioid Overdose

Pinpoint pupils → ↓ BP → Respiratory depression/arrest → Pulmonary edema → Stupor → Coma
NCLEX Alert: Antidote = Naloxone (Narcan) IV/IM. Critical: Duration of naloxone is shorter than heroin → repeated doses may be needed. Do NOT leave the patient unattended — they can lapse back into coma rapidly.

Cocaine Overdose

Cocaine is a potent CNS stimulant. Overdose can be life-threatening.

↑ HR → ↑ BP → Hyperpyrexia → Seizures → Ventricular arrhythmias

Management: Airway support, seizure control (benzodiazepines), cardiac monitoring, treat hyperthermia. If ingested: activated charcoal; body packers may need whole bowel irrigation.

Alcohol Withdrawal / Delirium Tremens

  • DTs usually start 48-96 hours after last drink
  • Life-threatening if untreated
  • Symptoms: anxiety, fear, tremor, agitation, insomnia, visual/tactile/auditory hallucinations
  • Autonomic overactivity: tachycardia, dilated pupils, hyperthermia, profuse perspiration
  • Treatment: benzodiazepines; CIWA protocol for assessment
  • Monitor VS every 30 minutes with DTs
Common Mistake: Assuming a patient is "just drunk." Always rule out head injury, hypoglycemia (mimics intoxication), and other medical conditions before attributing behavior to intoxication.
Pause & Think: Why are benzodiazepines the drug of choice for alcohol withdrawal?

Alcohol and benzodiazepines both act on GABA receptors in the brain. During chronic alcohol use, the brain adapts to constant GABA stimulation. When alcohol is suddenly removed, the brain is left in a hyperexcitable state. Benzodiazepines substitute for alcohol's GABA effect, preventing seizures, reducing agitation, and allowing the brain to gradually readjust. They also have anticonvulsant properties that protect against withdrawal seizures.

Sexual Assault

Management Goals

  • Provide support and reduce emotional trauma
  • Gather evidence for legal proceedings; provide privacy
  • Never leave patient alone
  • Use nonjudgmental approach

Physical Examination & Evidence

  • External evidence of trauma (bruises, contusions, lacerations)
  • Dried semen stains (crusted, flaking areas)
  • Broken fingernails; body tissue and foreign materials under nails
  • Oral examination with saliva specimen
  • Pelvic/rectal exams; perineum examined with Wood lamp (fluorescent = possible semen)
  • Use water-moistened vaginal speculum (lubricant interferes with forensic testing)
Key Insight: Each clothing item → paper bag (NOT plastic). Chain of custody maintained. If drug-facilitated assault suspected, urine drug test must be completed within 96 hours. STI prophylaxis and emergency contraception (within 12-24h, no later than 72h) should be offered.

"Use of force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of subjection into involuntary servitude." ED may be the only access point for healthcare.

Suicidal Patients

Attempted suicide stems from depression and can be viewed as a cry for help. Males are at greater risk for completed suicide; females attempt more often.

Risk Factors

  • Older adults, young adults, those under great stress
  • Unemployed, divorced, widowed, living alone
  • Previous suicide attempt or family history of suicide
  • Psychiatric illness
  • Communication of suicidal intent, specific plan, and means to carry out plan
Warning: Do NOT leave the patient alone. Crisis management focuses on treating injuries, determining suicidal potential, identifying areas of depression/conflict, evaluating support systems, and determining need for hospitalization or psychiatric referral.

Emergency Operations Plan (EOP)

Health care facilities are required by The Joint Commission to create an EOP and practice it twice yearly. The main goal is protection of the community.

Essential Components

Clinical Pearl: During a disaster, nurses may be asked to perform duties outside their area of expertise (intubation, chest tube insertion, suturing). Nurses must strive to maximize patient safety and be aware of state regulations related to nursing practice.

Pause & Think: A disaster occurs. Patient A has a tension pneumothorax (treatable). Patient B has penetrating head wounds and is unresponsive. Resources are scarce. Who gets priority?

Patient A gets priority (RED). A tension pneumothorax is life-threatening but survivable with minimal intervention (needle decompression). Patient B has extensive injuries with unlikely survival (BLACK/expectant) — in a disaster, scarce resources are directed to those most likely to survive. Patient B receives comfort measures.

Anthrax — Most Likely Weaponized Agent

Gram-positive, encapsulated rod. Odorless, invisible, travels great distances. Incubation: 1-6 days.

Skin (Most Common)
Edema, pruritus, macule/papule → ulceration with vesicles → painless eschar (falls off in 1-2 weeks)
Ingestion
Fever, N/V, abd pain, bloody diarrhea, possible ascites and sepsis. Targets terminal ileum and cecum.
Inhalation (Most Deadly)
Flu-like symptoms → brief recovery → severe respiratory distress, stridor, hypoxia, cyanosis, shock → death in 24-36h. Mortality approaches 100%.
NCLEX Alert: Hallmark of inhaled anthrax = hemorrhagic mediastinitis on chest X-ray. Inhaled anthrax can incubate up to 60 days. Treatment: penicillin (sensitive); mass exposure prophylaxis = oral ciprofloxacin or doxycycline × 60 days. Uses standard precautions only (does NOT spread person to person). After death, cremation is necessary (spores survive decades).
Pause & Think: Why doesn't inhaled anthrax respond to antibiotics once the second stage begins?

During the second stage, the bacteria have already released massive amounts of toxin. While antibiotics can kill the bacteria, they cannot neutralize the toxins already circulating. The toxins cause hemorrhage, edema, and necrosis that continue to damage tissues even after the bacteria are eliminated. This is why early recognition and treatment during the initial flu-like phase is critical.

Chemical Weapons

Effects are more apparent and occur more quickly than biologic weapons. Most chemicals are heavier than air (except hydrogen cyanide) — stand up to reduce exposure.

Pause & Think: Why does cyanide poisoning cause bright red skin?

Cyanide blocks the mitochondrial enzyme cytochrome oxidase, preventing cells from using oxygen. The oxygen remains in the blood because cells cannot extract it. The venous blood remains oxygenated (bright red) because the oxygen is never consumed at the cellular level. This gives the skin a characteristic bright red or cherry-red appearance.

Radiation Exposure

Types of Radiation-Induced Injury

External Irradiation
All/part of body exposed. Patient is NOT radioactive. Decontamination NOT necessary. Not a medical emergency.
Contamination
Exposure to radioactive gases/liquids/solids (external or internal). Requires immediate management to prevent incorporation.
Incorporation
Uptake of radioactive material into cells, tissues, organs. Target organs: kidneys, bones, liver, thyroid. Sequelae can occur days to years later.

Exposure Factors

Time — Minimize + Distance — Maximize + Shielding — Use barriers

Acute Radiation Syndrome

PhaseTimingS/S
Prodromal48-72h post-exposureN/V, loss of appetite, diarrhea, fatigue; high-dose: fever, respiratory distress
LatentAfter prodromal; up to 3 weeksSymptom-free. ↓ Lymphocytes, leukocytes, thrombocytes, RBCs
Manifest illnessAfter latent phaseInfection, fluid/electrolyte imbalance, bleeding, diarrhea, shock, altered LOC
Recovery or DeathAfter manifest illnessWeeks-months for recovery; ↑ ICP = sign of impending death

Decontamination

  • Triage outside hospital (prevent facility contamination)
  • Cover floors; seal air ducts and vents; strict isolation
  • Waste: double-bagged, labeled "radiation waste"
  • Staff PPE: water-resistant gowns, two pairs of gloves, caps, goggles, masks, booties
  • Dosimetry devices for all staff members
  • Decontaminate patients with soap and water; resurvey after each wash
  • Protect clean areas from contaminated runoff during showering
  • Irrigate wounds → cover with water-resistant dressing before total body decontam
  • Thyroid protection: Potassium iodide (KI) before or promptly after radioactive iodine intake
NCLEX Alert: Know the three exposure factors: time, distance, shielding. External irradiation does NOT make the patient radioactive — no special isolation needed. Contamination DOES require decontamination. KI protects the thyroid specifically from radioactive iodine.
Pause & Think: Why is the latent phase of acute radiation syndrome dangerous even though the patient feels fine?

During the latent phase, the bone marrow is being destroyed even though the patient appears well. Lymphocytes, leukocytes, thrombocytes, and RBCs are progressively declining. The patient is becoming profoundly immunocompromised and at risk for hemorrhage. When the manifest illness phase hits, they have no defenses against infection and no ability to clot — making this phase extremely dangerous. The deceptive "feeling fine" period can delay recognition of the severity of exposure.

NCLEX Takeaways

1. ED vs. Disaster Triage: In ED, sickest patient gets most resources. In disaster, do greatest good for greatest number. RED = immediate/survivable, BLACK = expectant/comfort only.
2. Primary Survey = ABCDE: Always airway first. Assume C-spine injury in ALL trauma. AVPU for quick neuro check. Ventilatory assessment before hemorrhage assessment (tension pneumo mimics hypovolemia).
3. Tourniquet: Document time applied. Do NOT remove until OR. Tag patient with "T" on forehead.
4. Carbon Monoxide: Pulse oximetry is NOT valid. Skin color NOT reliable. Treat with 100% O2 or hyperbaric until carboxyhemoglobin <5%.
5. Never Induce Vomiting: With corrosives (acids/alkalis) or petroleum distillates. Ipecac NOT recommended.
6. Acetaminophen: #1 overdosed drug. Antidote = NAC. Do NOT give with activated charcoal (charcoal absorbs NAC). Watch for hepatic failure in Stage III.
7. Naloxone (Narcan): Shorter duration than heroin → repeat doses. Never leave patient alone — rapid relapse to coma.
8. Delirium Tremens: 48-96h after last drink. Life-threatening. Treat with benzodiazepines. Monitor VS q30min.
9. Heat Stroke: Cool to 39°C within 1 hour. Stop at 38°C to prevent iatrogenic hypothermia. Cold water bath = optimal.
10. Hypothermia: Rewarm before defibrillation (ineffective <31°C). Watch for core temp afterdrop with peripheral rewarming. Cold blood returning = arrhythmia risk.
11. Rhabdomyolysis: Myalgias + muscle weakness + dark urine. CK >6000 = diagnostic. Watch for ATN/AKI.
12. Anthrax: Inhaled is most deadly. Hallmark = hemorrhagic mediastinitis on CXR. Standard precautions only. Prophylaxis = cipro or doxy × 60 days.
13. Nerve Agent (Sarin): SLUDGE symptoms. Treatment = atropine + pralidoxime + benzodiazepines.
14. Radiation: Time + distance + shielding. KI protects thyroid. Decontaminate with soap and water. External irradiation ≠ contamination.
15. Sexual Assault: Paper bags (not plastic). Water-moistened speculum. Chain of custody. Never leave alone. STI prophylaxis + emergency contraception.
16. Forensic Evidence: Paper bags, not plastic. Do not cut through existing tears/holes in clothing. Chain of custody is essential.
17. PPE Levels: A = highest (SCBA + vapor-tight suit). C & D = hospital levels. Don PPE BEFORE patient contact.
