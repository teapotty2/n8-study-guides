1. ECG Fundamentals üìë W1 Slides 22‚Äì40

1.1 What the ECG Measures

The electrocardiogram records the heart's electrical activity ‚Äî not mechanical pumping. It shows depolarization (electrical activation ‚Üí contraction) and repolarization (electrical recovery ‚Üí relaxation).

A normal ECG does NOT guarantee normal cardiac function. A patient can have a normal rhythm and still have poor cardiac output (e.g., pulseless electrical activity).

2. Systematic ECG Analysis üìë W1 Slides 41‚Äì46

2.1 The 12-Step Method

Always use a systematic approach ‚Äî never "eyeball" a rhythm:

  1. Determine ventricular rate
  2. Determine ventricular rhythm (regular or irregular?)
  3. Determine QRS duration ‚Äî consistent throughout?
  4. Identify QRS shape ‚Äî consistent or variable?
  5. Identify P waves ‚Äî is there a P before every QRS?
  6. Identify P-wave shape ‚Äî consistent?
  7. Determine atrial rate
  8. Determine atrial rhythm
  9. Determine each PR interval
  10. Are PR intervals consistent, patterned, or irregular?
  11. Determine P:QRS ratio (1:1?)
  12. Identify any ST changes or other abnormalities

Find an R wave on a dark line, label it "START," then count down for each subsequent dark line until the next R wave.

2.3 Rhythm Regularity

Measure the R-R intervals across the strip:

  • If R-R intervals are the same (or differ by <0.08 sec) = Regular
  • If R-R intervals vary = Irregular
  • Irregular but with a repeating pattern = Regularly irregular (e.g., Wenckebach)
  • Irregular with no pattern = Irregularly irregular (e.g., A-fib)

3. Electrolytes & the Heart üìë W1 Slide 38

Electrolytes are charged ions that control nerve impulses, muscle contraction, and cardiac rhythm. Even small imbalances can trigger lethal arrhythmias. You just learned in Section 1.8 how each ion maps to a part of the ECG ‚Äî now you'll see what happens when those ions go wrong.

Why electrolytes come before rhythms: Many arrhythmias you're about to study are caused by electrolyte imbalances. Understanding the ions first makes the rhythms logical instead of memorized. When you see "hypokalemia" listed as a cause of PVCs or V-Tach later, you'll already know why.

5 questions ‚ñº
Question 1 of 5
How can you tell sinus bradycardia from a junctional escape rhythm on the strip? Both are slow ‚Äî what's the key difference?
P waves. Sinus bradycardia has upright P waves before each QRS with normal PR interval (0.12‚Äì0.20). Junctional has inverted P waves (before, hidden in, or after QRS) or no P waves, and PR is < 0.12 if visible. Sinus=SA firing slowly. Junctional=AV junction has taken over.
Question 2 of 5
A-Fib vs A-Flutter ‚Äî what does each look like on the strip, and what's the key visual discriminator?
A-Fib: No P waves ‚Äî chaotic, fibrillatory baseline. Irregularly irregular rhythm.
A-Flutter: Sawtooth F-waves at ~300/min, usually with regular conduction ratio (2:1, 3:1, 4:1).
Key discriminator: sawtooth = flutter; chaotic baseline = fib.
Question 3 of 5
A patient has been in A-Fib for 3 days. Can you cardiovert them? What must happen first and why?
NO ‚Äî not without anticoagulation first. A-Fib > 48 hours allows blood to stagnate in the fibrillating atria ‚Üí clot formation. Cardioversion restores organized contraction ‚Üí can dislodge clots ‚Üí stroke. Must anticoagulate for ~3 weeks before cardioversion, or do a TEE to rule out atrial thrombus.
Question 4 of 5
Where can junctional P waves appear? List all 3 positions and what each looks like.
P waves in junctional rhythms can be: 1) Before QRS (inverted, short PR < 0.12), 2) Hidden IN the QRS (not visible ‚Äî buried), 3) After QRS (inverted, sitting in the ST segment). All because the impulse travels retrograde through the atria.
Question 5 of 5
First-line drug for symptomatic SVT? What's the nursing consideration that makes this drug unique?
Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push (followed by 12 mg if needed). Unique consideration: half-life is only 6‚Äì10 seconds, so it must be given via rapid push through a proximal IV, immediately followed by a 20 mL NS flush. Warn the patient they may feel chest tightness and a brief "sense of doom" ‚Äî it's normal and goes away in seconds.

7.4 Asystole & PEA üìë W1 Slides 67‚Äì70

8. Heart Blocks üìë W1 Slides 71‚Äì80

Heart blocks are conduction delays or failures at the AV node. They range from benign (1st degree) to life-threatening (3rd degree).

8.1 First-Degree AV Block üìë W1 Slide 72

1st Degree AV Block

Rate: Normal  |  Rhythm: Regular
PR: > 0.20 sec but CONSTANT  |  P:QRS: 1:1
Every P wave conducts ‚Äî just delayed

8.2 Second-Degree Type I (Wenckebach) üìë W1 Slide 73

"Longer, longer, longer, DROP ‚Äî that's a Wenckebach!"

Causes: Inferior MI, medications (digoxin, beta blockers)

8.3 Second-Degree Type II (Mobitz II) üìë W1 Slide 74

2nd Degree Type II ‚Äî Mobitz II ‚ö†Ô∏è DANGEROUS

Rate: Usually slow  |  Rhythm: May be regular or irregular

Type II is MORE dangerous than Type I. It occurs lower in the conduction system (Bundle of His) and can suddenly progress to complete heart block. Pacemaker usually required.

8.4 Third-Degree (Complete) Heart Block üìë W1 Slides 75‚Äì80

‚Ä¢ Transcutaneous pacing IMMEDIATELY
‚Ä¢ Atropine (may not work if block is below AV node)
‚Ä¢ Epinephrine or dopamine drip
‚Ä¢ Permanent pacemaker required
What does 3rd-degree block look like at the bedside? The patient is bradycardic (HR 20‚Äì40 is ventricular, 40‚Äì60 if junctional escape). They may be hypotensive, syncopal, or in cardiogenic shock. "Give atropine for 3rd-degree block" ‚Äî atropine works at the AV node, so it may help if the escape rhythm is junctional (narrow QRS). But if the block is infranodal (wide QRS escape), atropine is useless. Transcutaneous pacing is always the safest first-line.
‚Üí Conduction anatomy: SA ‚Üí AV ‚Üí Bundle ‚Üí Purkinje (Section 1.7). The level of the block determines the escape rate and QRS width.

Question 1 of 5

Question 3 of 5

"Longer, longer, longer, DROP ‚Äî that's a Wenckebach!"

Question 4 of 5

P waves and QRS fire completely independently ‚Äî no relationship between them ("AV dissociation"). The atria beat at their rate (60‚Äì100), ventricles at escape rate (20‚Äì40 bpm if infranodal). Question 5 of 5

Caused by prolonged QT interval (from hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia, or QT-prolonging drugs). On strip: "twisting of the points" ‚Äî QRS complexes rotate around the baseline, amplitude fluctuates. First-line treatment: IV magnesium sulfate (1‚Äì2 g). If pulseless, defibrillate.
