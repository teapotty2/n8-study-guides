Cardiac Diagnostic Testing

Lab Markers ‚Äî What Gets Drawn and Why

Lab What It Tells You Key Values
Troponin (I or T) Myocardial cell death ‚Äî the gold standard for MI detection. Rises 3-4 hrs after injury, peaks 12-24 hrs. Elevated = myocardial damage. Serial draws q3-6h to track trend.
CK-MB Cardiac-specific creatine kinase isoenzyme. Less specific than troponin but useful for reinfarction detection (clears faster). Rises 4-6 hrs, peaks 12-24 hrs, returns to normal 48-72 hrs.
BNP / NT-proBNP Released by stretched ventricular walls ‚Äî rises with volume overload. Distinguishes cardiac vs. pulmonary dyspnea. BNP >100 pg/mL suggests HF. Higher = worse HF.
Lipid Panel LDL, HDL, triglycerides, total cholesterol. Risk stratification for CAD. LDL <100 (or <70 if high risk). HDL>40M / >50F.
CRP / hs-CRP Inflammatory marker ‚Äî elevated CRP correlates with atherosclerotic disease activity. hs-CRP >3 mg/L = higher cardiovascular risk.
? A patient presents with chest pain. Troponin drawn on arrival is normal. Does this rule out MI?
No. Troponin takes 3-4 hours to rise after myocardial injury. A negative troponin on arrival means nothing ‚Äî you need serial troponins drawn every 3-6 hours. The trend matters more than a single value. This is why patients with chest pain get admitted for serial labs even when the first one is clean.
? Patient is short of breath. BNP comes back at 850 pg/mL. What does this tell you vs. a BNP of 45?
BNP 850 = almost certainly heart failure causing the dyspnea. The ventricles are stretched and screaming. BNP 45 = the heart is probably fine ‚Äî look for a pulmonary cause (pneumonia, PE, COPD exacerbation). BNP is your tiebreaker between "is this the heart or the lungs?"

Non-Invasive Cardiac Tests

Test What It Shows Nursing Considerations
12-Lead ECG Electrical activity ‚Äî rhythm, conduction, ischemia/injury patterns (ST changes) Skin prep (clean, dry, shave if needed). Correct lead placement is critical for accurate reading.
Echocardiogram Ultrasound of heart ‚Äî chamber size, wall motion, valve function, EF Non-invasive, no special prep for transthoracic (TTE). TEE requires NPO and sedation.
Stress Test Heart's response to exercise ‚Äî reveals ischemia not visible at rest Hold beta-blockers and caffeine. Monitor BP, HR, ECG throughout. Stop if chest pain, significant ST changes, arrhythmias, or hypotension.
Pharmacologic Stress Test Same info as exercise stress ‚Äî for patients who can't exercise Uses adenosine, dipyridamole, or dobutamine to simulate exercise. Hold caffeine 24h (it blocks adenosine). Have aminophylline as reversal agent.
Holter Monitor Continuous ECG recording (24-48h) ‚Äî catches intermittent arrhythmias Patient keeps a diary of symptoms and activities. Don't get it wet. Report any symptoms with timestamps.
üß† Mental Model
Non-invasive tests answer the question: "What's going on?" ECG = electrical problem? Echo = structural/functional problem? Stress test = supply-demand mismatch when the heart works harder?
Each test targets a different dimension of cardiac function.

Cardiac Catheterization

What It Is

A catheter is threaded (usually via femoral or radial artery) into the heart to directly measure pressures, visualize coronary arteries with dye (angiography), and potentially intervene (angioplasty/stent ‚Äî PCI). This is the definitive diagnostic test for coronary artery disease.

  • Left-heart cath ‚Üí arterial approach ‚Üí visualizes coronary arteries, LV function, aortic/mitral valves, measures LV pressures
  • Right-heart cath ‚Üí venous approach ‚Üí measures RA, RV, PA pressures, PCWP (pulmonary capillary wedge pressure) ‚Üí assesses hemodynamics
? Why is checking for a contrast/iodine/shellfish allergy critical before cardiac cath?
The contrast dye used in angiography is iodine-based. Patients with iodine or shellfish allergies are at higher risk for anaphylaxis. If allergic, they need premedication with corticosteroids and antihistamines (e.g., prednisone + diphenhydramine) before the procedure. Also assess for metformin use ‚Äî it must be held 48 hours before/after because contrast + metformin ‚Üí risk of lactic acidosis.

Pre-Procedure Nursing Care

  • Consent ‚Äî verify informed consent is signed
  • Allergies ‚Äî iodine, shellfish, contrast dye, latex
  • Labs ‚Äî check BUN/Cr (kidneys must handle contrast), PT/INR (bleeding risk), CBC, type & screen
  • Meds ‚Äî hold metformin 48h pre/post (lactic acidosis risk). Anticoagulants per MD order. May continue aspirin.
  • NPO ‚Äî usually 6-8 hours before procedure
  • Baseline assessment ‚Äî mark peripheral pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) with a marker. Document baseline neuro status and skin color/temp of extremities.
  • IV access ‚Äî establish for fluid hydration (helps kidneys flush contrast)
  • Teaching ‚Äî warm flushing sensation when dye is injected is normal. May feel like they need to urinate. Must lie flat afterward.

Post-Procedure Nursing Care ‚Äî The Big Focus

  • Hemostasis at site ‚Äî apply pressure or use a closure device. Femoral: bed rest 2-6 hours, leg straight, HOB ‚â§30¬∞. Radial: shorter rest, but monitor hand perfusion.
  • Neurovascular checks q15min √ó 4 ‚Üí then q30min √ó 2 ‚Üí then q1h. Check the 5 P's: Pain, Pulselessness, Pallor, Paresthesia, Paralysis in the affected extremity.
  • Check insertion site ‚Äî bleeding, hematoma, ecchymosis. Mark any hematoma borders with a pen and recheck for expansion.
  • Distal pulses ‚Äî compare to baseline marks. Diminished or absent pulse = emergency ‚Üí notify MD immediately.
  • Hydration ‚Äî push oral fluids + IV fluids to flush contrast. Monitor urine output (‚â•30 mL/hr). Watch for contrast-induced nephropathy (rising BUN/Cr).
  • Vital signs ‚Äî watch for hypotension (bleeding, vasovagal), hypertension, arrhythmias.
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical ‚Äî Know This Cold
If the patient's distal pulse disappears post-cath, the catheter may have caused arterial occlusion or thrombosis. This is a surgical emergency. Hold pressure, notify the provider immediately, and do NOT elevate the leg (keeps blood flowing distally).
? Femoral cath patient wants to get up to use the bathroom 1 hour post-procedure. What do you do?
Deny the request ‚Äî kindly but firmly. Femoral access requires bed rest 2-6 hours with the affected leg straight and HOB ‚â§30¬∞. Offer a bedpan or urinal. Explain that bending at the groin could dislodge the clot at the insertion site and cause hemorrhage. This is non-negotiable.
? Why is metformin held for 48 hours after cardiac cath?
Contrast dye is nephrotoxic ‚Äî it can temporarily impair kidney function. Metformin is renally excreted. If the kidneys can't clear metformin properly, it accumulates ‚Üí lactic acidosis (potentially fatal). You hold it 48h and recheck renal function (BUN/Cr) before restarting. This is a classic exam question.

Hemodynamic Monitoring

The Mental Model

Hemodynamic monitoring = measuring pressures inside the cardiovascular system in real-time. It answers: Is the heart pumping enough? Is there enough volume? Is resistance too high/low?

Think of it as checking pressure at different points in a plumbing system to figure out where the problem is.

Key Hemodynamic Parameters

Parameter Normal What It Reflects
CVP (Central Venous Pressure) 2‚Äì6 mmHg Right atrial pressure ‚Üí preload of right heart. Reflects volume status.
PAP (Pulmonary Artery Pressure) Systolic: 20-30
Diastolic: 8-12
Mean: 10-20		 Pressure in pulmonary artery ‚Üí right heart afterload. Elevated in pulmonary HTN, ARDS, left HF.
PCWP / PAWP (Pulmonary Capillary Wedge) 8‚Äì12 mmHg Indirect measure of left atrial pressure ‚Üí left heart preload. The best single number for left ventricular function.
CO (Cardiac Output) 4‚Äì6 L/min Total blood pumped per minute. CO = SV √ó HR.
SVR (Systemic Vascular Resistance) 800‚Äì1200 dynes Left heart afterload. High SVR = vasoconstriction. Low SVR = vasodilation (sepsis).
üß† Mental Model ‚Äî Follow the Catheter
A PA (Swan-Ganz) catheter enters through a central vein and travels through: RA ‚Üí RV ‚Üí PA ‚Üí wedges in a pulmonary capillary branch. Each position measures the pressure at that point. When wedged, it "sees past" the pulmonary vasculature to reflect left atrial pressure ‚Äî that's why PCWP = left heart preload.
? CVP reads 12 mmHg (elevated). What does this likely indicate, and what conditions cause it?
Elevated CVP = right-sided volume overload or right heart failure. The right atrium is under more pressure than normal. Causes: fluid overload, right-sided HF, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism (anything that backs up blood into the right side). Nursing response: assess for JVD, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly. May need diuretics or fluid restriction.
? PCWP is 22 mmHg (normal 8-12). What's happening and what will you see clinically?
Elevated PCWP = left-sided heart failure. The left ventricle can't keep up, so pressure backs up into the pulmonary vasculature. Clinically you'll see: pulmonary edema (crackles on auscultation), dyspnea, orthopnea, pink frothy sputum in severe cases. When PCWP >18-20, fluid starts leaking into alveoli. Treatment: diuretics (furosemide), nitrates (reduces preload), oxygen, position upright.
? Septic shock patient: CO is high, SVR is low. Why does this pattern make sense?
Sepsis causes massive vasodilation (inflammatory mediators relax blood vessels) ‚Üí SVR drops. The heart compensates by pumping faster and harder ‚Üí CO goes up ("warm shock" / hyperdynamic phase). But despite high CO, tissues aren't getting adequate perfusion because the vascular tone is gone ‚Äî blood is pooling in dilated vessels rather than perfusing organs. Treatment: vasopressors (norepinephrine) to restore SVR, plus IV fluids to fill the expanded vascular space.

Arterial Line (A-line) Monitoring

  • Inserted into radial artery (most common) for continuous BP monitoring + arterial blood sampling
  • Perform Allen's test before insertion ‚Äî confirms adequate collateral circulation via ulnar artery
  • Level the transducer at the phlebostatic axis (4th ICS, midaxillary line) for accurate readings
  • Zero the system to atmospheric pressure ‚Äî eliminates drift in readings
  • Watch for: dampened waveform (air, clot, kink), accidental disconnection (arterial hemorrhage is rapid and fatal), infection at site
üíé Clinical Pearl
Allen's Test in 3 steps: Have patient make a fist. Occlude both radial and ulnar arteries. Release the ulnar artery only. If the palm flushes pink within 5-7 seconds ‚Üí positive (safe ‚Äî ulnar provides backup). If it stays white ‚Üí do NOT use that radial artery for an a-line.

Quick Self-Check ‚Äî Lecture Ready?

1. Post-cardiac cath, your patient develops sudden pain, pallor, and pulselessness in the right leg. Priority action?
Acute loss of pulse + pain + pallor = arterial occlusion. This is a surgical emergency. Keep leg flat (don't elevate ‚Äî you need gravity to help distal perfusion), apply pressure if bleeding, and notify the provider immediately. Every minute counts.
2. PCWP of 24 mmHg with crackles and dyspnea. Which medication addresses the underlying hemodynamic problem?
High PCWP = volume overload backing into the lungs. Furosemide (loop diuretic) reduces preload by removing excess fluid. Norepi would increase afterload (making it worse). Dopamine could increase HR but doesn't address the fluid problem. The crackles are literally fluid in the alveoli from the elevated pulmonary pressures.
3. Patient on metformin is scheduled for cardiac cath tomorrow. What medication teaching is essential?
Contrast dye is nephrotoxic. Metformin is renally cleared. Impaired kidneys + metformin = metformin accumulation = lactic acidosis (can be fatal). Hold metformin, hydrate well, recheck BUN/Cr before restarting. This is a board-favorite question.
4. Before inserting a radial arterial line, the nurse performs Allen's test. The patient's palm remains pale after releasing the ulnar artery. Next step?
A negative Allen's test (palm stays pale) means the ulnar artery cannot provide adequate collateral circulation to the hand. If you cannulate the radial artery and it occludes, the hand could become ischemic. Choose a different site (other wrist, brachial, or femoral artery).
5. CVP 1 mmHg, HR 122, BP 82/60. What hemodynamic picture does this suggest?
Low CVP = low preload = not enough volume returning to the heart. The heart compensates with tachycardia (HR 122) trying to maintain CO, but BP is still low because stroke volume is tanked. This patient needs volume ‚Äî IV fluids and/or blood products. Think: hemorrhage, severe dehydration, or third-spacing.
