1. Cardiac Foundations üìë W1 Slides 2‚Äì10

1.1 Cardiac Anatomy

The heart functions as a dual pump, with the right side managing pulmonary circulation and the left side driving systemic circulation. Understanding the structural differences between chambers explains why certain conditions affect one side preferentially.

The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the superior and inferior vena cava and directs it to the lungs via the pulmonary artery. The left atrium receives oxygenated blood from the pulmonary veins for distribution to the body. This arrangement means the right heart handles low-oxygen blood while the left heart handles oxygen-rich blood.

The left ventricle is 2-3 times thicker than the right ventricle because it must generate enough pressure to overcome systemic vascular resistance‚Äîpushing blood through the entire body. The right ventricle operates as a low-pressure system because pulmonary vascular resistance is much lower than systemic resistance. This thickness difference has clinical implications: the left ventricle is more susceptible to hypertrophy from chronic hypertension, while the right ventricle fails when pulmonary pressures rise (as in COPD or pulmonary embolism).

The heart is rotated within the chest so that the left ventricle lies closest to the chest wall. This positioning allows us to palpate the Point of Maximal Impulse (PMI) at the 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line. A displaced PMI suggests ventricular enlargement‚Äîif shifted laterally, suspect left ventricular hypertrophy or dilation.

1.2 Cardiac Conduction System üìë W1 Slide 3

The heart generates its own electrical impulses through specialized conduction tissue. This intrinsic automaticity means the heart can beat independently of nervous system input, though autonomic influences modulate rate and force.

The conduction pathway follows a precise sequence:

  1. SA Node (sinoatrial): The primary pacemaker, located in the right atrium. Intrinsic rate of 60-100 bpm. Sets the pace for the entire heart.
  2. Internodal pathways and Bachmann's bundle: Conduct impulse across atria
  3. AV Node (atrioventricular): Secondary pacemaker (40-60 bpm). Creates a brief delay allowing atrial contraction to complete before ventricular contraction begins.
  4. Bundle of His: Conducts impulse from AV node to ventricles
  5. Right and Left Bundle Branches: Divide to serve each ventricle
  6. Purkinje Fibers: Terminal network ensuring rapid, coordinated ventricular contraction. Intrinsic rate 20-40 bpm.
The pacemaker hierarchy matters clinically. If the SA node fails, the AV node takes over at a slower rate. If both fail, Purkinje fibers produce a very slow escape rhythm (20-40 bpm)‚Äîoften insufficient to maintain adequate cardiac output. This is why complete heart block is an emergency.

Electrical Properties of Cardiac Cells

Property Definition Clinical Relevance
Automaticity Ability to spontaneously generate electrical impulse Enhanced automaticity causes ectopic beats (PACs, PVCs)
Excitability Ability to respond to electrical stimulus Affected by electrolytes, medications, ischemia
Conductivity Ability to transmit impulse from cell to cell Impaired in heart blocks, bundle branch blocks

1.3 Action Potential and Refractory Periods üìë W1 Slides 4‚Äì5

Cardiac cells undergo depolarization and repolarization with each heartbeat. Understanding this cycle explains how antiarrhythmic drugs work and why certain timing matters for cardioversion.

Depolarization is electrical activation: sodium rapidly enters the cell while potassium exits, reversing the cell's electrical charge. This triggers contraction. Repolarization returns the cell to its resting state: potassium exits the cell rapidly (Phase 3), and then the Na+/K+ ATPase pump restores resting ion concentrations (Phase 4), allowing the muscle to relax.

The action potential has five phases:

  • Phase 0: Rapid depolarization (sodium influx)
  • Phase 1: Early rapid repolarization
  • Phase 2: Plateau phase (calcium influx balances potassium efflux)
  • Phase 3: Final repolarization (potassium efflux)
  • Phase 4: Resting membrane potential

Refractory Periods

Period What Happens Clinical Significance
Absolute (Effective) Refractory Period Cell cannot depolarize regardless of stimulus strength Protects heart from tetanic contraction; no stimulus can trigger a beat
Relative Refractory Period Cell can depolarize but requires stronger-than-normal stimulus Vulnerable period‚Äîdefibrillation here can induce arrhythmia
The relative refractory period corresponds to the T wave on ECG. Shocking during this vulnerable window can trigger ventricular fibrillation. This is why synchronized cardioversion uses SYNC mode‚Äîit times the shock to the R wave (absolute refractory period), avoiding the T wave.

1.4 The Cardiac Cycle üìë W1 Slides 6‚Äì7

Each heartbeat consists of precisely coordinated mechanical events driven by electrical activation. The cycle has three major phases:

Diastole (relaxation): All four chambers are relaxed. AV valves (tricuspid and mitral) are open, allowing blood to flow from atria to ventricles. Semilunar valves (aortic and pulmonic) are closed. Pressure in the chambers is at its lowest, facilitating passive ventricular filling. About 75% of ventricular filling occurs passively during early diastole.

Atrial Systole: The SA node fires, triggering atrial contraction. This "atrial kick" actively pushes the remaining 25% of blood into the ventricles. While 25% seems small, losing atrial kick (as in atrial fibrillation) significantly reduces cardiac output, especially when the heart rate is fast and diastolic filling time is shortened.

Ventricular Systole: Following atrial contraction, the ventricles contract. Rising ventricular pressure closes the AV valves (producing S1) and opens the semilunar valves, ejecting blood into the pulmonary artery and aorta.

Fluid always flows from high pressure to low pressure. Understanding this principle explains why valvular stenosis (narrowing) and regurgitation (backflow) disrupt normal hemodynamics. A stenotic valve creates a pressure gradient; a regurgitant valve allows backflow when pressure relationships reverse.

1.5 Cardiac Output: The Master Formula üìë W1 Slides 7‚Äì10

CO = SV √ó HR Cardiac Output (L/min) = Stroke Volume (mL/beat) √ó Heart Rate (beats/min) Normal: 4-6 L/min

Cardiac output is the total volume of blood pumped by the ventricle per minute. It represents the heart's ability to meet the body's metabolic demands. Normal CO is 4-6 L/min, but this can increase dramatically during exercise (up to 20-25 L/min in athletes).

Stroke volume is the amount of blood ejected with each heartbeat. Three factors determine SV:

Preload üìë W1 Slide 7

Preload is the degree of stretch on cardiac muscle fibers at the end of diastole‚Äîessentially, how much blood fills the ventricle before it contracts. It reflects left ventricular end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) and volume.

The Frank-Starling Law states: the greater the stretch (within physiologic limits), the stronger the contraction, and the greater the stroke volume. Think of it like stretching a rubber band‚Äîup to a point, more stretch means more snap-back force.

Preload Status Causes Clinical Action
Decreased Preload Hemorrhage, dehydration, diuretics, vasodilators, blood loss, fluid volume deficit "Fill the tank"‚ÄîIV fluids, blood products
Increased Preload Fluid overload, heart failure, renal failure, excessive IV fluids Diuretics, fluid restriction, vasodilators (nitrates)

Afterload üìë W1 Slide 7

Afterload is the resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood. For the left ventricle, this primarily reflects systemic vascular resistance (SVR). For the right ventricle, it reflects pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR).

There is an inverse relationship between afterload and stroke volume: increased afterload means the heart works harder but pumps less volume. This is why chronic hypertension (high afterload) leads to heart failure over time‚Äîthe heart eventually cannot overcome the resistance.

Increase Afterload Vasoconstriction increases resistance ‚Üí supports BP in shock Norepinephrine (Levophed), Vasopressin
ABC reduces afterload:
A ‚Äì ACE inhibitors and ARBs
B ‚Äì Beta-blockers
C ‚Äì Calcium channel blockers

1.6 Ejection Fraction üìë W1 Slide 8

Ejection fraction (EF) is the percentage of end-diastolic volume ejected with each heartbeat. It primarily refers to the left ventricle and serves as a key measurement of contractility and overall cardiac function.

Ejection Fraction Interpretation Clinical Significance
55-65% Normal Heart pumping efficiently
40-54% Mildly reduced Early dysfunction, may need monitoring
<40% Reduced (HFrEF) Indicator of heart failure; guides treatment
EF is measured via echocardiogram. A patient with EF <35% who survives a cardiac event often qualifies for ICD placement to prevent sudden cardiac death from ventricular arrhythmias.

1.7 Baroreceptors üìë W1 Slides 7, 10

Baroreceptors are specialized nerve cells located in the aortic arch and bilateral internal carotid arteries. They sense blood pressure changes and trigger compensatory responses:

Blood Pressure State Baroreceptor Response Result
Hypertension detected Increased firing rate to brainstem ‚Üì Heart rate, ‚Üì BP (parasympathetic activation)
Hypotension detected Decreased firing rate to brainstem ‚Üë Heart rate, ‚Üë BP, vasoconstriction (sympathetic activation)

This reflex arc explains why position changes can cause orthostatic hypotension in patients with impaired baroreceptor function (diabetic neuropathy, elderly patients, volume depletion).

1.8 Gerontologic Considerations üìë W1 Slide 11

Aging produces predictable changes in cardiac structure and function that affect how older patients present and respond to treatment:

Older adults often present with atypical symptoms: SOB, fatigue, and palpitations rather than classic chest pain. This delay in recognition contributes to worse outcomes.

1.9 Gender Differences üìë W1 Slide 11

Women have distinct cardiovascular profiles compared to men:

  • Smaller heart and narrower vessels make angioplasty technically more difficult
  • Estrogen provides protection: increases HDL, decreases LDL, promotes vasodilation, increases blood flow to heart
  • CAD onset delayed ~10 years compared to men due to estrogen protection
  • Risk equalizes post-menopause: Men at age 45 = Women at age 55 for CAD risk
  • Atypical presentations more common: Women often present with fatigue, nausea, back pain rather than classic crushing chest pain
Women, African Americans, and older adults are more likely to delay seeking care during MI due to atypical symptoms or dismissal of symptoms. This delay worsens outcomes. Educate patients that jaw pain, back pain, unusual fatigue, and nausea can be MI symptoms.

3.3 Systematic ECG Analysis üìë W1 Slides 41‚Äì43

Always use a systematic approach to avoid missing abnormalities:

  1. Rate: Calculate ventricular rate (and atrial rate if different)
  2. Rhythm: Regular or irregular? Regularly irregular or irregularly irregular?
  3. P waves: Present? One before each QRS? Consistent shape?
  4. PR interval: Normal (0.12-0.20)? Constant or varying?
  5. QRS: Width (<0.12)? Consistent shape?
  6. P:QRS ratio: 1:1? More P's than QRS's?

4. Cardiac Arrhythmias üìë W1 Slides 47‚Äì80

Arrhythmias are disorders of impulse formation, conduction, or both. They range from benign to immediately life-threatening. Understanding the origin and mechanism guides treatment.

4.1 Sinus Rhythms

Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)

Characteristic Value
Rate 60-100 bpm
Rhythm Regular
P waves Present, consistent shape, one before each QRS
PR interval 0.12-0.20 seconds, constant
QRS <0.12 seconds
P:QRS 1:1
An elevated resting heart rate (even within "normal" range) correlates with increased cardiovascular risk. A resting HR increase of 10+ bpm increases risk for sudden cardiac death, A-fib, CAD, and stroke.

Sinus Bradycardia

Rate: <60 bpm with otherwise normal sinus characteristics

Causes: Sleep, athletic training, hypothyroidism, vagal stimulation (vomiting, suctioning, bearing down), severe pain, increased ICP, medications (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin, antiarrhythmics)

Treatment (if symptomatic):

  • Unstable (hypotension, AMS, shock, ischemic chest pain): Atropine 0.5 mg IV every 3-5 minutes, max 3 mg
  • Persistent despite atropine: Transcutaneous pacing, dopamine or epinephrine infusion

Sinus Tachycardia

Rate: >100 bpm (usually <120) with normal sinus characteristics (P may be buried in preceding T wave)

Causes: Physiologic/psychological stress, acute blood loss, shock, hypo/hypervolemia, HF, fever, pain, exercise, caffeine, nicotine, amphetamines, cocaine

Treatment: Identify and treat the underlying cause (sinus tachycardia is a symptom, not a primary arrhythmia)

  • Address the trigger: fever, pain, hypovolemia, anxiety, etc.
  • If diagnosis is uncertain (may be SVT rather than sinus tach): vagal maneuvers or adenosine can help differentiate
  • IV beta-blockers or CCBs only when the underlying cause has been addressed and rate control is still needed

4.2 Atrial Arrhythmias

Premature Atrial Complex (PAC)

An early P wave that appears before the next expected sinus impulse. The early P wave may have a different shape than sinus P waves.

Causes: Caffeine, alcohol, nicotine, anxiety, hypokalemia, often seen with tachycardia

Patient may report: "My heart skipped a beat"

Treatment: Treat underlying cause. If >6/minute, may indicate worsening state or impending A-fib.

Atrial Fibrillation (A-fib) üìë W1 Slides 53‚Äì57

The most common sustained arrhythmia, affecting 2.7-6.1 million Americans.

Pathophysiology: Abnormal impulse formation creates rapid, disorganized, uncoordinated atrial activity (300-600 bpm atrial rate). The AV node filters these impulses, producing an irregular ventricular response (120-200 bpm untreated).

ECG Feature Finding
Atrial rate 300-600 bpm
Ventricular rate 120-200 bpm (uncontrolled)
Rhythm Irregularly irregular
P waves Absent; fibrillatory (f) waves instead
PR interval Cannot be measured
P:QRS Many:1

Hemodynamic Consequences:

  • Loss of atrial kick (25-30% of CO)
  • Rapid rate reduces ventricular filling time ‚Üí smaller SV
  • Blood stasis in fibrillating atria ‚Üí thrombus formation ‚Üí stroke risk

4.3 Ventricular Arrhythmias

Premature Ventricular Complex (PVC)

An impulse originating in the ventricle before the next normal sinus beat. The wide, bizarre QRS (≥0.12 seconds) reflects abnormal ventricular depolarization.

Causes: Caffeine, nicotine, alcohol, cardiac ischemia/infarction, digitalis toxicity, acidosis, hypokalemia

Patterns:

  • Bigeminy: Every other beat is a PVC
  • Trigeminy: Every 3rd beat is a PVC
  • Quadrigeminy: Every 4th beat is a PVC
  • Couplet: Two PVCs in a row
  • Monomorphic (unifocal): All PVCs look the same (one irritable focus)
  • Polymorphic (multifocal): PVCs have different shapes (multiple foci‚Äîmore concerning)

Ventricular Tachycardia (V-Tach) üìë W1 Slides 58‚Äì62

V-TACH IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY
Three or more PVCs in a row at rate >100 bpm = Ventricular Tachycardia
Feature Finding
Rate 100-200 bpm
Rhythm Usually regular
QRS ≥0.12 seconds, bizarre/abnormal shape
P waves Usually not visible (may see AV dissociation)

Risk factors: Low EF, large MI, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, CHF, valvular disease, hypoxemia

Treatment:

  • Monomorphic VT with pulse: Synchronized cardioversion
  • Pulseless VT: CPR, defibrillation, ACLS algorithm
  • Recurrent VT with EF <35%: ICD placement

Torsades de Pointes

A polymorphic VT with a characteristic "twisting of peaks" pattern. QRS complexes appear to twist around the isoelectric line.

Preceded by: Prolonged QT interval

Causes:

  • Medications: IV Haloperidol, fluoroquinolones (Cipro), azithromycin, lithium, methadone
  • Electrolytes: Hypokalemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia
  • Congenital long QT syndrome

Treatment:

  • IVPB Magnesium Sulfate (even if Mg level normal)
  • Correct underlying electrolyte abnormality
  • If pulseless: CPR, defibrillation, Mag sulfate

Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)

V-FIB = DEATH IS IMMINENT

Treatment: IMMEDIATE DEFIBRILLATION
Feature Finding
Rate >300 bpm (chaotic)
Rhythm Grossly irregular
QRS No recognizable QRS complexes

V-fib is the most common arrhythmia in cardiac arrest. Early defibrillation is the definitive treatment‚Äîevery minute of delay decreases survival by 7-10%.

Idioventricular / Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)

A ventricular escape rhythm that occurs when higher pacemakers (SA node, AV node) fail or are suppressed. Often seen as a reperfusion arrhythmia after successful thrombolytic therapy or PCI ‚Äî actually considered a good sign that blood flow has been restored.

Feature Finding
Rate Idioventricular: 20-40 bpm
Accelerated (AIVR): 40-100 bpm
Rhythm Usually regular
QRS Wide (≥0.12 seconds) ‚Äî ventricular origin
P waves Usually absent or dissociated

Treatment: Usually self-limiting and benign ‚Äî observe, do NOT suppress with antiarrhythmics (it may be the patient's only rhythm). Treat only if hemodynamically unstable. Atropine may help by speeding sinus rate to override the ventricular focus.

Post-reperfusion AIVR is a marker of successful treatment, not a complication. Do not treat a stable AIVR ‚Äî suppressing it could unmask an even slower or absent underlying rhythm.

Asystole

"Flatline"‚Äîabsence of any electrical activity. Confirm in two different leads to rule out lead disconnection.

Treatment: High-quality CPR, epinephrine, address reversible causes (H's and T's). Defibrillation is NOT indicated for asystole.

4.4 Conduction Blocks

First-Degree AV Block

All impulses conduct, but slower than normal through the AV node.

Feature Finding
PR interval >0.20 seconds but constant
P:QRS 1:1
QRS Usually normal

Causes: Beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin, inferior MI, aging

Treatment: Usually observation; review medications

Second-Degree AV Block Type I (Wenckebach/Mobitz I)

Progressive PR prolongation until a beat is dropped, then the cycle repeats.

Feature Finding
PR interval Gets progressively longer until dropped QRS
Pattern Repeating cycle
P:QRS 3:2, 4:3, 5:4, etc.
QRS Usually normal

Treatment: Usually benign; address underlying cause

Second-Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)

Some impulses conduct, some don't‚Äîwithout progressive PR lengthening. More dangerous than Type I.

Feature Finding
PR interval Constant for conducted beats
Dropped beats Sudden, without warning
P:QRS 2:1, 3:1, 4:1, etc.
QRS Usually wide/abnormal
Mobitz II can suddenly progress to complete heart block. These patients often need pacemakers.

5.1 Cardioversion vs. Defibrillation üìë W1 Slides 82‚Äì84

Both deliver electrical current to depolarize a critical mass of myocardial cells, allowing the SA node to recapture pacemaker function. The key difference is timing:

Feature Cardioversion Defibrillation
Timing SYNCHRONIZED with R wave UNSYNCHRONIZED (immediate)
SYNC mode ON OFF
Indications Organized tachyarrhythmias with pulse (A-fib, flutter, stable VT) V-fib, pulseless VT

Why SYNC mode matters: Shocking during the relative refractory period (T wave) can induce V-fib. SYNC mode ensures the shock is delivered during the absolute refractory period (R wave), avoiding this danger.

Safety Protocol for Cardioversion/Defibrillation

  1. Ensure good skin contact with pads/paddles
  2. Remove pads/paddles from medication patches and ensure no contact with oxygen source
  3. For women with large breasts: place left pad lateral to or under left breast
  4. Apply 20-25 lb pressure when using paddles
  5. Do NOT charge until ready to shock
  6. Call "CLEAR" THREE times with visual checks:
    • First: Check YOU are clear
    • Second: Check team members are clear
    • Third: Final visual sweep‚Äîeveryone clear
  7. Deliver shock, record energy level and resulting rhythm
  8. Resume CPR immediately after shock (start with compressions)
  9. After 5 cycles (~2 minutes), recheck rhythm
  10. Post-event: inspect skin for burns, restock supplies

  • Moderate sedation and analgesia

6. Coronary Artery Disease üìë W1 Slides 99‚Äì117

6.1 Atherosclerosis Pathophysiology

Atherosclerosis is the abnormal accumulation of lipids, fibrous tissue, calcium, and cellular debris within arterial walls. This process begins with endothelial injury and progresses over decades.

Progression:

  1. Endothelial injury (from HTN, smoking, hyperlipidemia)
  2. Lipid infiltration and inflammation
  3. Atheroma (plaque) formation
  4. Plaque growth narrows lumen ‚Üí reduced blood flow
  5. Unstable plaque (thin cap, inflammation) may rupture
  6. Rupture attracts platelets ‚Üí thrombus formation
  7. Thrombus occludes vessel ‚Üí ischemia ‚Üí necrosis (MI)
  8. Necrotic tissue replaced by scar ‚Üí cardiac dysfunction ‚Üí HF

CAD is the leading cause of death in the US across all racial/ethnic groups and the most prevalent cardiovascular disease in adults.

9.2 Why HF Gets Worse: The Compensatory Cascade

This is the most important concept in the HF section. When cardiac output drops, the body activates three compensatory mechanisms. They help short-term but cause long-term damage ‚Äî creating a vicious cycle that drives HF progression.

Why this matters for medications: Every major HF drug class targets a specific part of this cascade:
  • Beta-blockers (carvedilol, metoprolol) ‚Äî block SNS activation (mechanism 1)
  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs (lisinopril, valsartan) ‚Äî block RAAS at angiotensin conversion (mechanism 2)
  • Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone) ‚Äî block RAAS at the end: aldosterone-driven Na/H2O retention (mechanism 2)
  • ARNIs (Entresto) ‚Äî block RAAS + enhance the protective natriuretic peptide system
  • Diuretics (furosemide) ‚Äî directly remove the excess fluid caused by RAAS activation
  • Digoxin / Inotropes ‚Äî directly increase contractility to compensate for the weakened pump
You're not giving random drugs. You're interrupting a specific harmful cascade at specific points.

10.1 Cardiogenic Shock

10.2 Cardiac Tamponade üìë W2 Slides 48‚Äì50

Accumulation of fluid in pericardial space compresses the heart, restricting filling and reducing cardiac output. Normal pericardial fluid is ~20 mL. 12. Shock States üìë W2 Slides 97‚Äì127

Shock is a life-threatening condition of inadequate tissue perfusion. Without treatment, it progresses to multiple organ failure and death.

The unifying concept: Shock is always a perfusion problem. Something is broken in the system that delivers blood to tissues. To understand and treat shock, ask one question: Is the problem the pump, the volume, or the pipes?

12.1 Pump, Volume, Pipes: The Shock Framework

The treatment follows the broken piece. Pump broken? Strengthen it (inotropes). Tank empty? Fill it (fluids/blood). Pipes too wide? Tighten them (vasopressors). Something blocking? Remove it. If you can identify what's broken, you know the treatment direction ‚Äî even before you remember specific drugs.

12.4 Anaphylactic Shock

A severe, life-threatening Type I (IgE-mediated) hypersensitivity reaction causing massive vasodilation and increased capillary permeability. Onset within minutes of allergen exposure.

Common triggers: Medications (antibiotics, NSAIDs), foods (nuts, shellfish), insect stings/bites, latex, contrast dye

Pathophysiology: Allergen triggers massive histamine and leukotriene release ‚Üí widespread vasodilation (pipes wide open) + capillary leak (fluid escapes vessels) + bronchospasm (airway narrows)

Signs and symptoms:

  • Skin: Urticaria (hives), angioedema (facial/lip/tongue swelling), flushing, pruritus
  • Respiratory: Stridor, wheezing, dyspnea, laryngeal edema ‚Üí airway obstruction
  • Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia, weak pulse, shock
  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping, diarrhea
Anaphylaxis Treatment Priority:
1. EPINEPHRINE IM 0.3-0.5 mg (anterolateral thigh) ‚Äî FIRST and MOST IMPORTANT action
2. Call for help / activate rapid response
3. Position supine with legs elevated (unless respiratory distress)
4. High-flow oxygen; prepare for intubation if airway compromise
5. IV access, IV fluids (1-2 L NS bolus)
6. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV ‚Äî H1 blocker
7. Famotidine IV ‚Äî H2 blocker
8. Methylprednisolone IV ‚Äî prevents biphasic reaction
9. Albuterol nebulizer for bronchospasm

Epinephrine reverses ALL three pathologic processes simultaneously: vasoconstriction (counters vasodilation), bronchodilation (opens airways), and decreased capillary permeability (stops fluid leak). No other single drug does all three ‚Äî that's why it's always first.
