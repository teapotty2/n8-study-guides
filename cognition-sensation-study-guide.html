1. Neuroanatomy Foundations HEAVY IN SLIDES

Neurotransmitters: The Chemical Messengers

Neurotransmitters are the molecular currency of neurological communication. They're manufactured and stored in synaptic vesicles, then released when an electrical action potential reaches the nerve terminal. Once released, they cross the synapse and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic cell—either exciting or inhibiting that target cell. Understanding neurotransmitter imbalances is fundamental to understanding many neurological disorders.

Neurotransmitter Primary Source Action Clinical Correlation
Acetylcholine Many brain areas; parasympathetic NS Usually excitatory; sometimes inhibitory (vagal heart stimulation) Myasthenia Gravis: impaired ACh binding at NMJ
Dopamine Substantia nigra; basal ganglia Usually inhibitory; affects behavior, attention, fine movement Parkinson's Disease: decreased dopamine availability
Norepinephrine Brain stem; hypothalamus; sympathetic NS Usually excitatory; affects mood and overall activity Sympathetic "fight or flight" response
Serotonin Brain stem; hypothalamus; dorsal horn Inhibitory; controls mood, sleep, inhibits pain pathways Depression, sleep disorders
GABA Spinal cord; cerebellum; basal ganglia Inhibitory Seizure disorders (decreased GABA)
Enkephalin/Endorphin Spine; brain stem; thalamus; pituitary Excitatory; pleasurable sensation, inhibits pain Natural pain modulation

Brain Anatomy: The Four Lobes HEAVY IN SLIDES

The cerebrum—the largest part of the brain—is divided into two hemispheres connected by the corpus callosum. Each hemisphere contains four lobes, each with distinct functions. The convoluted surface (gyri and sulci) maximizes surface area, allowing for higher-level cognitive function.

Lobe Location Primary Functions Damage Manifestations
Frontal (largest) Anterior to central sulcus Concentration, abstract thinking, memory, motor function, judgment, personality, affect, inhibitions Personality changes, poor judgment, motor deficits
Parietal Posterior to frontal Sensory analysis and interpretation, body position awareness, size/shape discrimination, right-left orientation Sensory deficits, spatial disorientation
Temporal Inferior to frontal and parietal Auditory reception, memory of sound, understanding language and music Hearing deficits, receptive aphasia (Wernicke's)
Occipital Most posterior Visual interpretation and memory Visual field deficits, visual processing problems

Critical Speech Centers

Broca's Area (Expressive Aphasia)

Location: Left inferior frontal gyrus

Function: Motor control of speech

If Damaged: Patient cannot speak or write BUT can understand language

Clinical Tip: Close to left motor area—right-sided paralysis often accompanies Broca's aphasia

Wernicke's Area (Receptive Aphasia)

Location: Left posterior temporal gyrus

Function: Language comprehension

If Damaged: Patient cannot understand language; speaks in stream of unconnected words

Clinical Tip: Speech sounds fluent but is nonsensical ("word salad")

Other Critical Structures

Brain Stem (midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata): Contains vital reflex centers for respiration, heart rate, blood pressure, coughing, vomiting, swallowing, and sneezing. The reticular formation here controls arousal and sleep-wake cycles. Motor fibers cross (decussate) at the medulla level—this is why a stroke on one side of the brain causes deficits on the opposite side of the body.

Cerebellum: Located posterior to the brain stem, below the occipital lobe. Responsible for smooth coordination of movement, fine motor control, balance, and proprioception—the awareness of body position without looking. Damage causes ataxia (uncoordinated movements).

Hypothalamus: Small but mighty. Regulates temperature, pituitary hormone secretion, fluid balance, hunger, sleep-wake cycles, blood pressure, and autonomic nervous system function. Connected to the posterior pituitary via the infundibulum.

The Meninges: Three Protective Layers HEAVY IN SLIDES

Herniation: When intracranial pressure exceeds the brain's compensatory capacity, brain tissue is compressed against or displaced around the dural folds (falx cerebri, tentorium). This is a life-threatening emergency that compresses vital brain stem structures.
Blood or abscess can accumulate in the potential spaces:
  • Epidural hematoma: Blood between skull and dura (usually arterial—middle meningeal artery)
  • Subdural hematoma: Blood between dura and arachnoid (usually venous—bridging veins)
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Blood in the subarachnoid space (aneurysm rupture, AVM)

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

Parameter Normal Value Clinical Significance
Appearance Clear and colorless Pink/bloody = subarachnoid hemorrhage; cloudy = infection
Specific gravity 1.007 —
Production rate ~500 mL/day Produced in choroid plexus of ventricles
Total volume 125-150 mL Circulates in ventricles and subarachnoid space
White blood cells Minimal (0-5/mm³) Elevated in meningitis
Red blood cells None Present in hemorrhage

CSF is absorbed by arachnoid villi—finger-like projections into the venous system. When blood or bacteria enter the subarachnoid space, these villi become obstructed, causing communicating hydrocephalus. Blockage within the ventricular system itself causes obstructive hydrocephalus.

Cerebral Circulation: Unique Features HEAVY IN SLIDES

The brain receives approximately 15% of cardiac output—about 750 mL of blood per minute—despite comprising only 2% of body weight. It cannot store oxygen or glucose, making continuous blood flow absolutely essential.

Three Unique Features of Cerebral Circulation:

  1. Arterial and venous vessels are NOT parallel (unlike other organs)—due to venous system's role in CSF absorption
  2. Circle of Willis provides collateral circulation—allows blood flow redirection if one vessel is occluded
  3. Blood vessels have TWO layers instead of three—makes them more prone to rupture under pressure (aneurysm formation)
The Circle of Willis bifurcations are the most common sites for cerebral aneurysm formation. These outpouchings develop due to vessel wall weakness and can rupture, causing hemorrhagic stroke.

Cerebral Veins

Unlike veins elsewhere in the body, cerebral veins have NO VALVES. They depend entirely on gravity and blood pressure for flow, which is why positioning (head elevation, neutral alignment) is so critical in managing intracranial pressure.

The Blood-Brain Barrier

Formed by tight junctions between capillary endothelial cells and astrocytes, this barrier protects the CNS from many blood plasma substances—including many medications and antibiotics. The barrier can be disrupted by trauma, cerebral edema, and hypoxemia, which has implications for medication selection in CNS disorders.

2. Neurologic Assessment HEAVY IN SLIDES

Health History: Common Neurologic Symptoms

  • Pain and headache
  • Seizures
  • Dizziness (abnormal sensation of imbalance/movement) vs. Vertigo (illusion of rotation—the room is spinning)
  • Visual disturbances
  • Weakness
  • Abnormal sensations (paresthesias, numbness)

Components of the Neurologic Examination

Component What to Assess
Consciousness & Cognition Mental status, intellectual function, thought content, emotional status, language ability, impact on lifestyle
Cranial Nerves 12 pairs—test each according to function (see cranial nerve table)
Motor System Muscle size, tone, strength; coordination and balance; Romberg test
Sensory System Tactile sensation, superficial pain, temperature, vibration, proprioception
Reflexes DTRs (biceps, triceps, brachioradialis, patellar, Achilles), superficial, pathologic (Babinski)

The Babinski Reflex SLIDES

Stroke the lateral plantar surface of the foot. Normal response in adults: toes flex downward. Positive Babinski (toes fan out and big toe extends upward): indicates upper motor neuron lesion affecting the corticospinal tract. Note: Babinski is normal in infants and disappears by 9-10 months as the corticospinal tract matures.

Gerontologic Considerations SLIDES

Distinguishing normal aging from pathology is crucial in older adults:

Normal Aging Changes Clinical Implications
Brain weight decreases, ventricle size increases Increased risk for chronic subdural hematoma (brain atrophy stretches bridging veins)
Cerebral blood flow and metabolism reduced Slower mental function—not to be confused with dementia
Temperature regulation less efficient Higher risk of hypothermia/hyperthermia
Decreased visual/hearing acuity; taste bud atrophy Safety concerns, nutritional issues
Thirst sensation decreased Dehydration risk
DTRs may be absent or decreased Don't automatically interpret as pathologic
Reduced pain perception May not report symptoms; injuries go unnoticed
Careful with hot and cold packs! Reduced temperature and pain perception in older adults means they may not feel when something is too hot or too cold, risking burns or frostbite.
Know the difference: Changes in cognition (memory, language, judgment) are NOT normal parts of aging and always require investigation. Mental processing may slow, but significant memory lapses indicate pathology, not age.

3. Diagnostic Tests SLIDES

Test Purpose Key Nursing Considerations
CT Scan Quick structural imaging; differentiates ischemic vs hemorrhagic stroke Done within 20 min of ED arrival for stroke; client must lie still; check for contrast allergy
MRI Superior tissue detail; detects demyelination, diffuse axonal injury Remove ALL metal; contraindicated with pacemakers; loud noises
PET Scan Metabolic activity; can detect neurotransmitter levels May cause dizziness, lightheadedness, headache
EEG Records brain electrical activity; diagnoses seizures, brain death Avoid caffeine; wash hair after (lubricant used); hold anticonvulsants as ordered
Lumbar Puncture Obtains CSF for analysis CONTRAINDICATED with increased ICP (risk of herniation); void beforehand
Cerebral Angiography Visualizes blood vessels; identifies aneurysms, AVMs Check for contrast allergy; assess puncture site post-procedure

Lumbar Puncture Complications

  • Rare but serious: Herniation of intracranial contents, spinal epidural abscess/hematoma, meningitis
  • Common: Temporary voiding problems, slight fever, backache/spasms, neck stiffness
  • Post-LP headache: Lessens when lying down; managed with analgesics, hydration, caffeine, supine position

4. Altered Level of Consciousness HEAVY IN SLIDES

LOC is the MOST SENSITIVE indicator of neurological status. It's always the first thing to change with deterioration and the most reliable measure of the patient's condition. A change in LOC, however subtle, demands immediate attention.

The LOC Continuum

State Description Key Feature
Alert/Conscious Awake, oriented, responds appropriately Normal
Confused Disoriented, impaired judgment Early warning sign
Lethargic Drowsy but arousable Requires stimulation to respond
Obtunded Difficult to arouse, limited responses Sleeps unless stimulated
Stupor Responds only to vigorous stimulation Requires painful stimuli
Coma Unconscious, unarousable, unresponsive No response to any stimuli

Special States of Altered Consciousness

State Description Distinguishing Feature
Akinetic Mutism Unresponsive to environment, no movement or sound May open eyes
Persistent Vegetative State Devoid of cognitive function Has sleep-wake cycles
Locked-In Syndrome Cannot move or respond Eye movements intact (pons lesion); cognition preserved

Motor Response Assessment: Posturing HEAVY IN SLIDES

"Decorticate = Cord still works" (flexion—bringing arms toward core)
"Decerebrate = Brain stem Damage" (extension—brainstem level injury)
Flaccid = worst prognosis (complete loss of motor function)

Nursing Care for Altered LOC

The overarching goal is to compensate for the patient's loss of protective reflexes and assume responsibility for total patient care while maintaining dignity and privacy.

Priority Interventions

Airway (FIRST PRIORITY):

  • Frequent respiratory monitoring and lung auscultation
  • HOB elevated 30 degrees
  • Lateral or semi-prone positioning
  • Suctioning, oral hygiene, CPT

Tissue Integrity:

  • Turning schedule, correct body alignment
  • Passive ROM
  • Splints, foam boots, trochanter rolls
  • Eye care: cotton balls with saline, artificial tears, eye patches (use cautiously—cornea may contact)
  • Scrupulous oral care

Thermoregulation:

  • For fever: minimal bedding, acetaminophen, hypothermia blanket, cooling sponge bath, fan
  • Monitor frequently; prevent shivering (increases ICP and metabolic demands)

Sensory Stimulation:

  • Talk to and touch patient; encourage family to do the same
  • Maintain day-night pattern
  • Orient frequently
  • Exception: When arousing from coma, patient may have agitation period—minimize stimulation during this time

5. Increased Intracranial Pressure HEAVY IN SLIDES

The Monro-Kellie Hypothesis

The skull is a rigid, closed container with fixed volume. Three components compete for this space:

Brain Tissue
(~1400g)
+
Blood
(~75 mL)
+
CSF
(~75 mL)
=
Constant Volume

If one component increases, at least one other must decrease—or ICP rises. Normal compensation involves shifting/displacing CSF, increasing CSF absorption, or decreasing cerebral blood volume. When these mechanisms fail, ICP begins to climb.

Normal ICP: 0-10 mmHg (upper limit 15 mmHg)

Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) HEAVY IN SLIDES

CPP = MAP - ICP

Mean Arterial Pressure minus Intracranial Pressure

CPP Value Clinical Significance
70-100 mmHg Normal
70-80 mmHg Target range to maintain adequate cerebral blood flow
< 50 mmHg IRREVERSIBLE NEUROLOGIC DAMAGE
ICP = MAP CESSATION OF CEREBRAL CIRCULATION

Autoregulation and CO₂

Autoregulation is the brain's ability to maintain constant cerebral blood flow despite changes in systemic blood pressure. This mechanism can be impaired by pathologic increases in ICP.

CO₂ Level Vascular Response Effect on ICP
↓ CO₂ (hypocapnia) Vasoconstriction ↓ ICP (but may cause ischemia)
↑ CO₂ (hypercapnia) Vasodilation ↑ ICP

Clinical Manifestations of Increased ICP

EARLY (Most Sensitive): Changes in LOC—restlessness, confusion, slowing of speech, delayed responses. The earliest sign!
EARLY: Pupillary changes, impaired extraocular movements (pressure on CN II, III, IV, VI)
PROGRESSING: Headache (constant, increasing, worse with movement), projectile vomiting
PROGRESSING: Weakness on one side (pyramidal tract compression)
LATE: Cushing's Triad—↑ BP with widened pulse pressure, ↓ HR (bradycardia), irregular respirations
LATE: Posturing (decorticate → decerebrate → flaccid)
OMINOUS: Loss of brain stem reflexes (pupillary, corneal, gag, swallowing)—approaching death
Cushing's Triad (↑ systolic BP with widening pulse pressure + bradycardia + irregular respirations) is a LATE sign indicating brain stem compression. By this point, herniation is imminent. The goal is to detect and treat increased ICP BEFORE this triad appears.

Nursing Interventions for Increased ICP HEAVY IN SLIDES

Positioning

  • HOB 30-45 degrees to promote venous drainage
  • Head in neutral (midline) position—avoid extreme rotation or flexion of neck (compresses jugular veins)
  • Avoid hip flexion (increases intra-abdominal pressure)

Avoid Activities That Increase ICP

  • Valsalva maneuver (straining)—use stool softeners
  • Coughing, sneezing (suction cautiously—preoxygenate first)
  • Abdominal distention
  • Emotional stress, frequent arousal from sleep
  • Clustering nursing activities (space interventions)

Maintain Calm Environment

  • Minimize noise, conversation, environmental stimuli
  • Protect patient from stress
  • Dim lighting if photophobia present

Monitoring

  • Monitor fluid status carefully—hourly I&O during acute phase
  • Strict aseptic technique for ICP monitoring system
  • ICP should not increase above 25 mmHg during nursing interventions and should return to baseline within 5 minutes

Medical Management of Increased ICP

Intervention Mechanism Nursing Considerations
Mannitol (osmotic diuretic) Pulls fluid from brain tissue into vascular compartment Monitor serum osmolality, electrolytes; watch for heart failure, pulmonary edema
Hypertonic saline (3%) Draws water out of brain tissue Monitor sodium levels closely
Dexamethasone (corticosteroid) Reduces edema surrounding brain tumors NOT effective for traumatic edema
CSF drainage (ventriculostomy) Directly reduces CSF volume Strict aseptic technique; monitor for infection (meningitis signs)
Hyperventilation (controversial) ↓ CO₂ causes vasoconstriction Risk of cerebral ischemia; PaCO₂ should not go below 30 mmHg
Sedation/Barbiturates Reduces metabolic demands Lorazepam, midazolam don't affect CBF; use pentobarbital for refractory ICP
Temperature control Fever increases cerebral metabolism Aggressive fever management; prevent shivering

Complications of Increased ICP

SIADH

Cause: Excessive ADH secretion

Presentation: Water retention, ↓ urine output, dilutional hyponatremia, serum hypo-osmolality

Treatment: Fluid restriction (<800 mL/day); 3% saline for severe cases

Diabetes Insipidus

Cause: Decreased ADH secretion (posterior pituitary)

Presentation: Massive urine output (dilute, odorless), hypernatremia, serum hyperosmolality

Treatment: Fluid replacement, desmopressin (synthetic ADH)

Post-craniotomy: Urine output >200 mL/hour for 2 consecutive hours suggests diabetes insipidus. This is a key assessment!

6. Intracranial Surgery SLIDES

Types of Procedures

Procedure Description Purpose
Craniotomy Opening of the skull (bone flap created and repositioned) Remove tumor, relieve elevated ICP, evacuate blood clot, control hemorrhage
Craniectomy Excision (removal) of a portion of skull Decompression for refractory ICP; bone not replaced initially
Cranioplasty Repair of cranial defect Uses plastic or metal plate to cover skull defect
Burr Holes Circular openings made in skull Exploration, ventricle access, shunting, aspirate hematoma/abscess
Transsphenoidal Approach Access through nasal cavity and sphenoid sinus Pituitary adenoma removal; avoids craniotomy

Transsphenoidal Approach: Specific Considerations SLIDES

  • Direct access to sella turcica with minimal trauma/hemorrhage
  • Risks: Transient diabetes insipidus (posterior pituitary manipulation), CSF leakage, visual disturbances, postop meningitis, SIADH
  • Post-op care: Nasal packing in place, no nose blowing, oral care per protocol, HOB elevated

Postoperative Care HEAVY IN SLIDES

Goals: Detect and reduce cerebral edema, relieve pain, prevent seizures, monitor ICP and neurologic status

Warning Signs of Increasing ICP Post-Op:
  • ↑ BP with ↓ HR and respiratory failure = increased ICP
  • Patient doesn't wake up or deteriorates = potential clot
  • Dilated pupil on operative side = intracranial hematoma
Cerebral edema peaks 24-36 hours post-surgery. A slight decrease in LOC on postoperative day 1-2 may represent this peak rather than a new complication—but always assess thoroughly.

Postoperative Complications

  • Increased ICP—monitor neuro status q15-60 min
  • Bleeding and hypovolemic shock
  • Fluid and electrolyte disturbances—monitor serum Na+, osmolality
  • SIADH or Diabetes Insipidus
  • Infection—change blood-stained dressings (medium for bacterial growth)
  • CSF leak—do NOT cough, sneeze, or blow nose
  • Seizures—anticonvulsants given pre- and post-surgery
  • VTE—early ambulation, SCDs

7. Seizures HEAVY IN SLIDES

A seizure is an abnormal episode of motor, sensory, autonomic, or psychic activity resulting from sudden, uncontrolled electrical discharge from cerebral neurons. Epilepsy is a chronic disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures.

Classification

Type Description Examples
Focal (partial) Originates in ONE hemisphere Focal aware, focal impaired awareness
Generalized Engages BOTH hemispheres from onset Tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic
Provoked Related to acute, reversible conditions Metabolic disturbances, infection, drug withdrawal

Clinical Manifestations SLIDES

  • Range from single staring episode (absence seizure) to full body involvement (generalized tonic-clonic)
  • Tongue often chewed
  • Uncontrolled release of urine and feces
  • Postictal phase: Confusion, sleepiness lasting hours; patient may not remember the event

Nursing Management During a Seizure HEAVY IN SLIDES

Priority: Safety and Documentation

During the seizure:

  • Stay with the patient
  • Protect from injury—clear area, loosen restrictive clothing
  • Do NOT restrain, do NOT put anything in mouth
  • Turn to side when possible to prevent aspiration
  • Time the seizure

Document:

  • Circumstances before seizure (was there an aura?)
  • Type of body movement
  • Pupil size
  • Incontinence (yes/no)
  • Duration
  • Ability to speak after
  • Cognitive status after

Seizure Interventions SLIDES

  1. Initiate seizure precautions, protect airway
  2. Check blood glucose
  3. Review labs for renal function
  4. Apply O₂ via NC; increase to maintain SpO₂ >90%
  5. Administer lorazepam 2 mg IV stat—may repeat x1 after 2 minutes if seizure continues
  6. Notify MD ASAP
  7. Get STAT labs
Post-Seizure Considerations: Status epilepticus and prolonged seizures can cause rhabdomyolysis (skeletal muscle breakdown). This can lead to acute kidney injury in 15% of patients. Monitor for hyperuricemia. Hydration and alkalinization help protect kidney function.

Status Epilepticus HEAVY IN SLIDES

MEDICAL EMERGENCY: Continuous seizure activity lasting ≥5 minutes OR serial seizures without recovery between episodes. Causes permanent brain damage from repeated anoxia.

Management:

  • Stop seizure ASAP; treat underlying causes (fever, stopping anticonvulsants, infection)
  • Establish advanced airway
  • IV anticonvulsants, electrolytes, glucose
  • EEG monitoring
  • Continuous cardiac and respiratory monitoring
  • Suction at bedside

Seizure Precautions

  • Padded side rails
  • Bed in lowest position
  • Suction equipment at bedside
  • Oxygen available
  • IV access maintained
  • Clutter-free environment

9. Cerebrovascular Disorders: Stroke HEAVY IN SLIDES

Stroke = "Brain Attack"

5th leading cause of death in the US

Leading cause of serious long-term disability

TIME IS BRAIN: 1.9 million neurons lost per minute; brain ages 3.6 years per hour without treatment

Types of Stroke

Ischemic Stroke (87%)

Cause: Obstruction (thrombus or embolism)

Mechanism: Decreased blood flow → anaerobic respiration → lactic acid → acidosis → cell death

Penumbra: Ischemic tissue around infarction that may be salvaged with timely intervention

Hemorrhagic Stroke (13%)

Cause: Bleeding into brain tissue, ventricles, or subarachnoid space

Common causes: Hypertension (primary), ruptured aneurysm, AVM

Mortality: Up to 50%

Clinical Manifestations: BEFAST HEAVY IN SLIDES

Letter Sign
BBalance problems
EEyes (visual changes)
FFace drooping
AArm weakness
SSpeech difficulty
TTime to call 911

Key Terminology SLIDES

Term Definition
HemiplegiaParalysis of one side of body
HemiparesisWeakness of one side
DysarthriaImpaired speech from muscle paralysis (slurred speech)
ApraxiaInability to perform previously learned actions
Expressive AphasiaCannot express oneself (speak/write)
Receptive AphasiaCannot understand language
HemianopsiaBlindness in half of visual field
AgnosiaLoss of object recognition (visual, auditory, tactile)
Why does a stroke on one side cause deficits on the opposite side? Upper motor neurons decussate (cross) at the level of the medulla. Damage above this level affects the contralateral (opposite) side of the body.

TIA (Transient Ischemic Attack) SLIDES

Temporary neurologic deficit from temporary blood flow impairment. Resolves within 24 hours (most <1 hour). Often called a "warning stroke"—3-15% of strokes are preceded by a TIA. Requires diagnostic workup!

Risk Factors SLIDES

Nonmodifiable Modifiable
Age >55 years Hypertension (PRIMARY RISK FACTOR)
African American, Hispanic Atrial fibrillation
Gender Diabetes
Smoking, excessive alcohol
Obesity, sedentary lifestyle
Elevated cholesterol/hematocrit
Sleep apnea (↓ brain blood flow, ↑ BP)
Atrial fibrillation is the most common cardiac cause of embolic stroke. Blood pools in the fibrillating atria, forming clots that can travel to the brain.

Ischemic Stroke Medical Management HEAVY IN SLIDES

Initial Diagnostic: Non-contrast CT within 20 minutes of ED arrival to differentiate ischemic vs hemorrhagic (determines treatment)

tPA (Tissue Plasminogen Activator) HEAVY IN SLIDES

Goal: Door-to-Needle within 45 minutes

Dosing: 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg)

10% as bolus over 1 minute

Remainder over 1 hour with 50 mL NS flush

Timing Eligibility
Within 3 hours Standard eligibility criteria
Extended to 4.5 hours EXCLUDES: age >80, NIHSS >25, history of DM + prior stroke, on oral anticoagulant
Too late CONTRAINDICATED—revascularization of necrotic tissue increases cerebral edema and hemorrhage
Major Risk of tPA: BLEEDING
  • Insert all invasive lines BEFORE administration
  • No anticoagulants for 24 hours
  • Monitor for bleeding (stool, IVs, change in LOC)

Post-tPA Monitoring

  • Assess q15 min for 2 hours
  • q30 min for 6 hours
  • q1 hour for 16 hours (total 24 hours)
  • Repeat CT and MRI

Permissive Hypertension

BP may be allowed up to 185/105 to perfuse the penumbra region. Don't aggressively lower BP unless extremely elevated—the brain needs that pressure to maintain perfusion.

Hemorrhagic Stroke HEAVY IN SLIDES

Cardinal symptom: Sudden, severe headache—"worst headache of my life"

Also: Early sudden LOC changes, nausea/vomiting, seizures, nuchal rigidity (if subarachnoid)

Vasospasm SLIDES

Serious complication occurring 4-14 days after subarachnoid hemorrhage (often 7-8 days when clot lyses). Vasospasm narrows the arterial lumen, reducing blood flow and causing secondary stroke.

Management:

  • Nimodipine—calcium channel blocker; dilates arterial smooth muscle; given to ALL patients with subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • Triple-H Therapy—Hypervolemia, Hypertension, Hemodilution (controversial; current guidelines favor euvolemia)

Aneurysm Precautions HEAVY IN SLIDES

Goal: Non-stimulating environment to prevent ICP increases and further bleeding

Aneurysm Precautions

  • Absolute bed rest with HOB 30 degrees
  • Avoid all activity that may increase ICP or BP
  • Avoid Valsalva maneuver, acute neck flexion/rotation
  • Stool softeners and mild laxatives
  • Non-stimulating environment: dim lighting, no reading, no TV, no radio
  • Visitors restricted
  • DVT prevention: intermittent pneumatic compression devices

Stroke Nursing Care SLIDES

Acute Phase Assessment

  • LOC (most sensitive indicator)
  • Motor symptoms, speech, pupil changes
  • I&O, BP maintenance, bleeding, O₂ saturation

Rehabilitation Focus

  • Exercise unaffected side; regular exercise routine
  • Sit upright for meals; chin tuck for swallowing; thickened liquids if needed
  • Scheduled voiding; bowel program with fiber/fluid
  • Communication board for aphasia
  • Early mobilization when stable
  • Passive ROM of paralyzed extremity 4-5 times daily

10. Head Injury / Traumatic Brain Injury HEAVY IN SLIDES

Most common cause of death from trauma. Falls are the #1 cause of brain trauma. Highest risk groups: children 0-4, adolescents 15-19, adults 65+.

Types of Injury

Primary Injury Secondary Injury
Direct contact to head/brain at moment of initial injury Evolves over hours/days after initial injury
Contusions, lacerations, skull fractures, hematomas, concussion Caused by cerebral edema, ischemia, chemical changes
Usually immediate and fixed PREVENTABLE with good care

Brain Injury Types SLIDES

Type Description Key Features
Concussion (Mild TBI) Temporary LOC, no structural damage Repeated concussions → CTE (chronic traumatic encephalopathy)
Contusion More severe with possible surface hemorrhage Effects peak 18-36 hours; longer unconsciousness
Diffuse Axonal Injury Widespread axon damage Immediate coma; decorticate/decerebrate posturing; diagnosed by MRI
Coup-Contrecoup Acceleration-deceleration injury Damage at impact site AND opposite side of brain

Intracranial Hematomas HEAVY IN SLIDES

Type Location Classic Presentation Key Points
Epidural Between skull and dura Brief LOC → LUCID INTERVAL → rapid deterioration EMERGENCY! Usually arterial (middle meningeal artery); requires immediate surgery
Subdural (Acute) Between dura and brain Symptoms develop over 24-48 hours; LOC, pupillary changes, hemiparesis Often after fall; high mortality; requires craniotomy
Subdural (Chronic) Between dura and brain Develops over weeks to months; fluctuating symptoms Older adults at high risk (brain atrophy); may mimic stroke
Intracerebral Within brain substance Varies by location May not be surgically accessible

Skull Fracture Signs SLIDES

Finding Significance
Battle Sign Ecchymosis (bruising) over the mastoid process—indicates basilar skull fracture
Raccoon Eyes Periorbital ecchymosis—indicates basilar skull fracture
Halo Sign Ring of clear fluid around blood stain on dressing—indicates CSF leak

Management Principles HEAVY IN SLIDES

ASSUME CERVICAL SPINE INJURY until it is ruled out by imaging. Immobilize at scene; one person stabilizes head and neck to prevent flexion, rotation, or extension.

Goals:

  • Preserve brain homeostasis
  • Prevent secondary injury
  • Treat cerebral edema
  • Maintain cerebral perfusion
  • Maintain oxygenation

Supportive Measures:

  • Respiratory support (intubation if needed)
  • Seizure precautions and prevention
  • NGT/OGT for reduced gastric motility
  • Sedation with benzodiazepines (lorazepam, midazolam)—don't affect CBF or ICP

Brain Death SLIDES

Cardinal Signs:

  • Coma
  • Absence of brain stem reflexes
  • Apnea

Apnea Test: Ventilated patient taken off ventilator with O₂ cannula delivering O₂ at 4-10 L/min. Absence of respirations with CO₂ rising to 60 mmHg (or 20 mmHg above baseline) over 8-10 minutes confirms brain death.

Brain death = cardiac death; patient must be taken off life support.

11. Spinal Cord Injury HEAVY IN SLIDES

294,000 persons in US live with SCI disability. MVAs are the leading cause. Males account for 78% of SCIs.

Most frequent injury levels: C5-C7, T12, L1 (areas of greatest mobility)

Term Definition
Paraplegia Paralysis of lower body
Tetraplegia (Quadriplegia) Paralysis of all four extremities
Complete lesion Total loss of sensory and motor function below level
Incomplete lesion Some function preserved below level

Spinal Shock vs Neurogenic Shock HEAVY IN SLIDES

Spinal Shock Neurogenic Shock
Sudden depression of reflex activity below injury level (areflexia) Loss of autonomic function below lesion
Muscular flaccidity, lack of sensation and reflexes Loss of sympathetic tone
↓ BP, ↓ HR ↓ BP, ↓ HR, ↓ cardiac output
Paralytic ileus (occurs 2-3 days post-SCI; resolves 3-7 days) Venous pooling (peripheral vasodilation)
MAP maintained at 85 mmHg during hyperacute phase WARM skin (unlike hypovolemic shock)
Hypotension with BRADYCARDIA (not tachycardia)
In neurogenic shock: Skin is WARM and DRY, heart rate is SLOW (bradycardia). This is the opposite of hypovolemic shock where skin is cool/clammy and heart rate is fast.

Autonomic Dysreflexia HEAVY IN SLIDES

ACUTE EMERGENCY! Occurs in patients with lesions ABOVE T6 after spinal shock has resolved (may occur years after injury).

Pathophysiology: Noxious stimulus below the level of injury triggers exaggerated sympathetic response that cannot be modulated due to the spinal cord lesion.

Symptoms

  • Severe, pounding headache
  • Sudden, severe hypertension
  • Profuse diaphoresis above lesion
  • Flushing above lesion, pallor below
  • Nasal congestion
  • Bradycardia

Most Common Triggers

  • Distended bladder (MOST COMMON)—kinked catheter, blocked drainage
  • Constipation, fecal impaction
  • Skin stimulation (pressure injury, tight clothing)

Nursing Interventions HEAVY IN SLIDES

  1. SIT THE PATIENT UP (lowers BP using gravity)
  2. Rapid assessment to identify and eliminate cause:
    • Check catheter for kinks/blockage—irrigate or replace
    • Check rectum for fecal impaction
    • Examine skin for irritation
  3. Administer ganglionic blocking agent: hydralazine IV if BP remains severely elevated
  4. Label chart that patient is at risk for autonomic dysreflexia
  5. Educate patient on prevention and management

SCI Nursing Care SLIDES

Respiratory (High Priority with High Injuries)

  • Monitor carefully for respiratory failure
  • Pulse oximetry and ABGs
  • Early, vigorous pulmonary care
  • Suction with caution; assisted coughing

Mobility

  • Maintain proper body alignment
  • PROM at least 4 times daily—contractures develop rapidly!
  • Monitor BP with position changes
  • Splints to prevent footdrop
  • DVT prevention: anticoagulation within 72 hours for at least 3 months

Leading Causes of Death in SCI Patients

  • Pneumonia
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Sepsis (often from pressure injuries or UTIs)

12. Meningitis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Inflammation of the meninges and fluid space surrounding the brain and spinal cord.

Type Cause Key Features
Bacterial (Septic) S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis More severe; requires immediate antibiotics; can be fatal
Viral (Aseptic) Enteroviruses; secondary to cancer or HIV More common in summer/early fall; generally self-limiting
Cryptococcal Cryptococcus fungus Most common in AIDS patients

Clinical Manifestations SLIDES

  • Headache and fever (high throughout illness)
  • Nuchal rigidity (stiff, painful neck)
  • Positive Kernig sign—with thigh flexed on abdomen, leg cannot be fully extended
  • Positive Brudzinski sign—neck flexion causes flexion of hips and knees (more sensitive)
  • Photophobia
  • Petechial or purpuric rash (especially with N. meningitidis)
  • Changes in LOC, behavioral changes

Management SLIDES

Prevention: Meningococcal vaccine at age 11-12 with booster at 16; first-year college students and military

Treatment:

  • Early high-dose IV antibiotics (Penicillin G with ceftriaxone for bacterial)
  • Dexamethasone (reduces inflammatory response)
  • Treatment for dehydration, shock, seizures
  • Close contacts: Antimicrobial prophylaxis within 24 hours (rifampin, cipro, Rocephin)

Nursing: Infection control precautions; frequent neuro assessment; pain and fever management; monitor for increased ICP

13. Autoimmune Neurologic Disorders HEAVY IN SLIDES

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Progressive immune-related demyelination of the CNS. Frequently relapsing-remitting pattern with exacerbations and periods of stability. Peak onset 25-50 years; women affected more than men. No cure; 7-14 year shorter life expectancy.

Manifestations:

  • Fatigue, weakness, numbness
  • Difficulty with coordination, loss of balance
  • Pain, visual disturbances
  • Spasticity (90% of patients), often in lower extremities

Diagnosis: Multiple plaques in CNS on MRI

Treatment:

  • Disease-modifying therapies: interferons, glatiramer acetate, IV methylprednisolone
  • Symptom management: baclofen or dantrolene for spasticity

Nursing Care:

  • Avoid extreme fatigue and strenuous activity
  • Maintain temperate environment—heat worsens symptoms; use air conditioning
  • Bladder/bowel programs
  • Fall prevention; assistive devices

Myasthenia Gravis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Autoimmune disorder where antibodies attack acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, impairing nerve-to-muscle communication.

Initial Manifestations:

  • Ocular muscles first: diplopia and ptosis (drooping eyelid)
  • Weakness of facial muscles
  • Dysphonia (voice impairment), dysphagia
  • Generalized weakness (worsens with use, improves with rest)

Diagnosis:

  • Tensilon Test: IV edrophonium → immediate improvement in muscle strength = positive test
    • Have atropine available for side effects (bradycardia, asystole, bronchoconstriction)
  • Ice Test (alternative when Tensilon contraindicated—cardiac issues/asthma): Ice pack over eyes for 1 minute → ptosis temporarily resolves

Treatment:

  • Mestinon (pyridostigmine)—first-line; inhibits acetylcholine breakdown at NMJ
  • Immunosuppressants, IVIG, plasmapheresis
  • Thymectomy—typically in patients <60 years; can result in complete remission; earlier = better outcomes. Monitor post-op in ICU for respiratory depression

Myasthenic Crisis vs Cholinergic Crisis

Myasthenic Crisis Cholinergic Crisis
Undermedication or disease exacerbation Overmedication with cholinesterase inhibitors
Often triggered by respiratory infection Too much medication
Both present with: Severe generalized weakness, respiratory/bulbar weakness → may require intubation
Avoid sedatives and tranquilizers in myasthenia patients—they can cause respiratory and cardiac depression. Monitor respiratory status closely; patients may need mechanical ventilation.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) HEAVY IN SLIDES

Autoimmune attack on peripheral nerve myelin. 60-70% follow a viral infection 1-3 weeks prior (CMV, Epstein-Barr, H. influenzae, Mycoplasma). Peak severity within 2-4 weeks. Most patients recover completely.

Manifestations:

  • Weakness, paralysis, paresthesia (numbness, "pins and needles")
  • Starts in lower extremities and ASCENDS upward
  • Pain, diminished or absent reflexes
  • Autonomic dysfunction: tachycardia, bradycardia, hypertension, hypotension
  • Bulbar weakness (can't swallow or clear secretions)
GBS is a medical emergency. Elective intubation BEFORE autonomic dysfunction occurs improves outcomes. Respiratory failure is the major life-threatening complication.

Treatment:

  • ICU with continuous monitoring
  • Respiratory support
  • Plasmapheresis or IVIG to reduce circulating antibodies

Nursing Focus:

  • Monitor vital capacity and negative inspiratory force
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring (autonomic dysfunction)
  • DVT prevention: compression devices, SCDs
  • Passive ROM at least 2x daily
  • Assess swallowing and gag reflex—aspiration precautions
  • Communication planning (patient may be unable to speak)

14. Cranial Nerve Disorders SLIDES

Trigeminal Neuralgia (Tic Douloureux)

Condition of CN V (trigeminal nerve) characterized by paroxysms of severe, stabbing facial pain. Probable cause: vascular compression. More common in 5th-6th decade, women, and patients with MS.

Triggers: Washing face, brushing teeth, eating, draft of air—any facial stimulation. Patients may avoid eating, neglect hygiene, and isolate themselves.

Treatment:

  • Antiseizure medications: carbamazepine (Tegretol), gabapentin (Neurontin), phenytoin
  • Baclofen (antispasmodic)
  • Surgical: microvascular decompression, radiofrequency coagulation

Bell's Palsy

Unilateral inflammation of CN VII (facial nerve) causing sudden facial paralysis.

Manifestations:

  • Unilateral facial weakness/paralysis with facial distortion
  • Increased lacrimation
  • Difficulty with speech and eating

Key Point: Most patients recover completely in 3-5 weeks; rarely recurs.

Treatment:

  • Corticosteroids within 72 hours
  • Eye protection (shield at night, artificial tears, sunglasses)
  • Facial exercises and massage
  • Reassure patient this is NOT a stroke

Key Formulas and Priority Concepts

Parameter Normal Value Critical Threshold
ICP 0-10 mmHg >15 mmHg requires intervention
CPP = MAP - ICP 70-100 mmHg <50 mmHg = irreversible damage
GCS 15 ≤8 = coma (airway protection needed)
Cerebral blood flow ~750 mL/min (15% CO) <25 mL/100g/min = ischemia
CSF volume 125-150 mL Production: 500 mL/day

Top Priority Nursing Concepts

  1. Airway always first—especially with altered LOC
  2. LOC is the most sensitive indicator—first thing to change with deterioration
  3. ICP management: HOB 30-45°, neutral head, avoid Valsalva
  4. Prevent secondary injury—edema peaks 24-36 hours post-injury
  5. Assume C-spine injury in all trauma until ruled out
  6. Time is brain: Door-to-needle 45 min for tPA
  7. Autonomic dysreflexia = emergency: Sit up, find cause (usually bladder)
  8. Respiratory failure: Major concern in GBS, myasthenia, high SCI
