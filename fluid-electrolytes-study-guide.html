1. Diabetes Mellitus: Foundations SLIDES

1.1 Pathophysiology & Classification

Diabetes mellitus is a group of metabolic diseases characterized by hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Approximately 34.1 million Americans have diabetes, and roughly one-third remain undiagnosed. Prevalence is increasing across all age groups, with minority populations and older adults disproportionately affected.

Understanding the why behind the classification matters because it determines everything downstream—which complications threaten the patient, which treatments work, and which emergencies to anticipate.

Type 1 Diabetes

Beta-cell destruction (autoimmune or idiopathic) leads to absolute insulin deficiency. Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells, and the body turns to fat for fuel—a metabolic cascade that, when uncontrolled, produces ketoacidosis. Onset is typically acute, often presenting with the "three Ps" (polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia) alongside sudden weight loss. Type 1 patients are insulin-dependent for life.

Type 2 Diabetes

A dual defect of insulin resistance and progressive beta-cell dysfunction accounts for approximately 95% of adult diabetes. The body produces insulin but not enough to overcome tissue resistance. Onset is gradual, often diagnosed incidentally through routine labs. DKA is uncommon in Type 2; instead, uncontrolled Type 2 progresses toward HHS—a slower, more insidious, and often deadlier emergency.

Other Classifications

  • LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults): autoimmune destruction presenting in adulthood, often misclassified as Type 2 initially
  • Gestational diabetes: insulin resistance driven by placental hormones, resolves postpartum but signals future Type 2 risk
  • Secondary diabetes: associated with other conditions (Cushing syndrome, pancreatitis, medications)
Clinical Pearl
COVID-19 and diabetes have a dangerous bidirectional relationship. Diabetic patients who contract COVID have significantly higher rates of intubation and death. The inflammatory cascade of severe COVID also appears to trigger new-onset diabetes in some patients.

1.2 Insulin Physiology & Therapy SLIDES

Insulin is the master regulatory hormone of metabolism. When it works properly, it does far more than lower blood glucose—it orchestrates the storage and utilization of all three macronutrients:

  • Transports glucose into cells for energy
  • Stimulates glycogen storage in liver and muscle
  • Signals the liver to stop releasing stored glucose
  • Promotes dietary fat storage in adipose tissue
  • Accelerates amino acid transport into cells
  • Inhibits breakdown of stored glucose, protein, and fat

When insulin is absent (Type 1) or insufficient (Type 2), every one of these functions fails simultaneously. The body is swimming in glucose it cannot use, breaking down fat and protein in desperation, and generating toxic byproducts in the process.

Safety Alert
Regular insulin is the ONLY insulin that can be given intravenously. In DKA, a continuous regular insulin drip is standard. Never give any other insulin type IV—the kinetics are unpredictable and dangerous.

Complications of Insulin Therapy

Local allergic reactions, systemic allergic reactions, and insulin lipodystrophy (lumpy fatty deposits or atrophied areas at injection sites from repeated use of the same spot) are all possible. Site rotation prevents lipodystrophy, and return demonstration is the gold standard for verifying patient technique.

Morning Hyperglycemia: Three Causes, Three Different Fixes

When a patient wakes up with high blood glucose, the instinct is to increase insulin. But the cause matters enormously because the wrong adjustment can make things worse. The diagnostic move is checking blood glucose at bedtime, 3 AM, and upon waking.

Memory Anchor
Somogyi = Swing. Blood sugar swings low, then bounces high. The treatment is less insulin, not more—counterintuitive but critical. If you increase insulin in a Somogyi patient, you make the overnight hypoglycemia worse and the rebound even higher.

1.3 Oral & Non-Insulin Agents SLIDES

Oral agents are used in Type 2 diabetes when diet and exercise alone are insufficient. While hypoglycemia is a concern with sulfonylureas and secretagogues, most other classes (metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors, TZDs, GLP-1 agonists, DPP-4 inhibitors) carry low standalone hypoglycemia risk. Combination therapy is common because each class targets a different piece of the metabolic puzzle.

Safety Alert — Metformin & Contrast Dye
Metformin must be held 48 hours before AND after any procedure involving iodinated contrast dye. The combination can precipitate lactic acidosis, especially in patients with any degree of renal impairment. Always check renal function (BUN, creatinine) before restarting.

1.4 Glucose Monitoring & Self-Management SLIDES

Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) is the cornerstone of diabetes management. The ADA recommends maintaining an HgbA1C below 7%, which correlates with an estimated average glucose of roughly 154 mg/dL. The five pillars of diabetes management work together: nutritional therapy, exercise, glucose monitoring, pharmacologic therapy, and education.

Nutritional Therapy

Meal planning considers food preferences, lifestyle, and cultural background. General guidelines target carbohydrates at 50–60% of calories (emphasizing whole grains), fat below 30% (saturated fat below 10%), and cholesterol under 300 mg/day. The glycemic index matters: combining starches with protein or fat slows absorption, and whole fruits are preferred over juice because the fiber blunts the glucose spike.

Exercise Considerations

Exercise lowers blood glucose, aids weight loss, reduces cardiovascular risk, and manages stress. But it requires careful insulin adjustment. Moderate exercise can cause delayed hypoglycemia hours later, while intense exercise (weightlifting, sprinting) triggers adrenaline release, which paradoxically raises blood glucose through counter-regulatory hormone effects and may require more insulin. A 15g carbohydrate snack before and after moderate exercise helps prevent hypoglycemia.

Clinical Pearl
When assessing patient readiness for diabetes self-management education, check readiness to learn before launching into teaching. A newly diagnosed patient who is overwhelmed, in pain, or grieving the diagnosis is not going to retain insulin injection technique. Prioritize "survival skills" first: recognizing hypo/hyperglycemia, when to call for help, and basic medication administration.

2. Acute Complications of Diabetes HEAVY IN SLIDES

These three emergencies—hypoglycemia, DKA, and HHS—represent the most immediately life-threatening complications of diabetes. The lecture slides devoted more coverage to this section than any other, and practice questions test it from multiple angles. The ability to differentiate between them, initiate correct treatment, and understand the underlying physiology is non-negotiable.

2.1 Hypoglycemia HEAVY IN SLIDES

Hypoglycemia occurs when blood glucose drops below 70 mg/dL, with clinically significant hypoglycemia defined at below 54 mg/dL. The three most common causes are too much insulin or oral hypoglycemic agent, too much physical activity, and not enough food. The clinical presentation follows a predictable neurologic deterioration as glucose drops lower.

Severity Classification

SeverityBG RangeSymptomsMechanism
Mild54–70 mg/dLSweating, tremor, tachycardia, palpitations, nervousness, hungerAdrenergic (sympathetic nervous system activation)
ModerateBelow 54 mg/dLInability to concentrate, headache, confusion, memory lapses, slurred speech, drowsiness, numbness of lips/tongueCNS glucose deprivation (neuroglycopenia)
SevereWell below 54 mg/dLDisorientation, seizures, loss of consciousness, deathSevere cerebral glucose deprivation
Safety Alert — Hypoglycemic Unawareness
Patients with autonomic neuropathy (common in long-standing diabetes) lose the adrenergic warning signs—no sweating, no tremor, no tachycardia. They go straight from "feeling fine" to confused or unconscious. This is called hypoglycemic unawareness, and these patients need tighter monitoring and education about frequent glucose checks.

Management of Hypoglycemia

Conscious patient (Rule of 15): Give 15–20 grams of fast-acting concentrated carbohydrate: 3–4 glucose tablets, or 4–6 ounces of juice or regular soda (never diet). Recheck blood glucose in 15 minutes. If still below 70 mg/dL or symptoms persist beyond 10–15 minutes, repeat treatment. Once glucose normalizes, provide a snack of protein plus complex carbohydrate unless a meal is planned within 30–60 minutes.

Unconscious or unable to swallow (BG < 54 mg/dL):

  • In hospital: IV push 25–50 mL of D50W (50% dextrose)
  • Outside hospital: SQ or IM glucagon 1 mg. Position on side (glucagon causes nausea; risk of aspiration when consciousness returns)
Memory Anchor
Conscious = Carbs. Unconscious = D50 (hospital) or Glucagon (community). Never try to put juice into the mouth of an unconscious patient—aspiration risk is immediate and lethal.

2.2 Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) HEAVY IN SLIDES

DKA is the result of absent or grossly inadequate insulin, leading to simultaneous metabolic catastrophe: hyperglycemia, dehydration, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis. It is most common in Type 1 diabetes but can occur in poorly managed Type 2 during severe physiologic stress. The three main precipitating events are missed insulin doses, illness or infection, and undiagnosed/untreated diabetes.

The DKA Cascade

Safety Alert — The Potassium Trap in DKA
Serum potassium may look normal or even elevated on admission because acidosis drives K+ out of cells into the blood. But total body potassium is depleted from osmotic diuresis. As you correct the acidosis with insulin and fluids, K+ shifts back into cells and serum levels plummet. If you are not aggressively replacing potassium, the patient can develop fatal hypokalemia and cardiac arrest. K+ must be maintained ≥ 4 mEq/L. Replace even if levels appear normal. Withhold replacement ONLY if hyperkalemic or not urinating.

DKA Treatment Protocol

  1. Rehydration FIRST (before correcting hyperglycemia with insulin):
    • 0.9% NS at 500–1000 mL/hr for first 2–4 hours
    • Then 0.45% NS at 200–500 mL/hr for several more hours
    • Total: 6–10 liters may be needed
    • When BG reaches 250–300 mg/dL, switch to D5W to prevent precipitous drop
  2. IV Regular Insulin:
    • Continuous drip at ~5 units/hr
    • Flush insulin through entire IV tubing first (insulin adheres to plastic)
    • Monitor BG hourly (POCT); when at target × 4 consecutive hours, move to q2h
    • Do NOT stop insulin drip until SQ insulin has been started
  3. Electrolyte Replacement:
    • K+ replacement: up to 40 mEq/hr may be needed. Add 20 mEq/L to IVF when K+ < 4
    • ECG monitoring q2–4h initially for first 8 hours
    • Monitor Na+, K+, Cl-, Ca, Mg, Phos, BUN, creatinine q6h
  4. Reverse Acidosis:
    • Insulin itself reverses acidosis (stops ketone production)
    • Bicarbonate infusion generally AVOIDED—it precipitates further K+ drops
    • BG often corrects before acidosis; insulin drip may continue 12–24h until bicarb ≥15–18 mEq/L

Monitoring priorities: BG, renal function, urine output, ECG, electrolytes, VS, lung sounds (fluid overload risk). Use caution in patients with HF or renal failure—the aggressive fluid resuscitation needed for DKA can tip them into pulmonary edema.

Anion Gap SLIDES

The anion gap helps confirm metabolic acidosis in DKA. Normal anion gap is 8–12 mEq/L (without K+) or 12–16 mEq/L (with K+). In DKA, ketoacid accumulation widens the gap significantly. A closing anion gap during treatment signals that acidosis is resolving.

Pseudohyponatremia in DKA SLIDES

Hyperglycemia creates hyperosmolality, which pulls water from the intracellular space into the extracellular space, diluting serum sodium. The sodium level on the lab report is falsely low. Use the correction formula:

Corrected Na+ = Measured Na+ + [1.6 × (Glucose - 100) / 100]

Sick Day Rules SLIDES

  • NEVER eliminate insulin doses when ill, even if not eating—illness and stress hormones raise blood glucose
  • Test BG more frequently (every 3–4 hours)
  • Test urine for ketones if BG > 300 mg/dL
  • Take supplemental fluids every hour if possible
  • Call provider if: BG > 300 mg/dL × 2 readings, positive ketones, unable to keep fluids down, illness lasting > 1–2 days
Clinical Pearl
Practice questions test sick day rules from multiple angles because patients consistently get this wrong in real life. The most common fatal mistake is skipping insulin because "I'm not eating." Stress hormones from illness, infection, or surgery raise blood glucose even without food intake. The insulin is still needed—it just may need dose adjustment.

2.3 Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar Syndrome (HHS) HEAVY IN SLIDES

HHS is the metabolic emergency of Type 2 diabetes. Unlike DKA, the body produces enough insulin to prevent fat breakdown and ketosis, but not enough to prevent hyperglycemia. The result is an insidious, progressive osmotic diuresis that plays out over days to weeks, culminating in profound dehydration, dangerous hyperosmolality, and neurologic deterioration.

HHS typically occurs in older adults with Type 2 diabetes (sometimes undiagnosed), often triggered by a physiologic stressor: stroke, infection, or medications that worsen hyperglycemia (thiazide diuretics). Because there is no ketoacidosis and no GI symptoms, the patient does not feel acutely ill—they tolerate the polyuria and polydipsia until neurologic changes finally bring them to medical attention. By then, dehydration is severe.

HHS Clinical Features

  • Blood glucose: 600–1200+ mg/dL
  • Serum osmolality: > 320 mOsm/L (vs. normal 275–295)
  • Arterial pH: normal (no acidosis)
  • Ketones: absent or minimal
  • Profound dehydration, hypotension, tachycardia
  • Variable neurologic signs from cerebral dehydration: confusion, lethargy, seizures, hallucinations, coma
  • HIGH MORTALITY RATE: 5–16% (compared to <1% for DKA)

HHS Treatment

Fluid and electrolyte replacement is the FIRST priority—even before insulin. The extreme dehydration is more dangerous than the hyperglycemia. Normal saline or half-normal saline (depending on sodium level and volume status) is given aggressively with central venous or hemodynamic pressure monitoring to guide volume. Insulin plays a less important role than in DKA because there is no acidosis to reverse. After recovery, many patients can control their diabetes with medical nutrition therapy alone or with oral agents.

Memory Anchor
DKA = Dry, Ketotic, Acidotic. Young Type 1 patient, rapid onset, fruity breath, Kussmaul breathing.
HHS = High sugar, Hyperosmolar, Sneaky. Older Type 2 patient, slow onset, no ketones, altered mental status is the hallmark. Higher mortality because patients present later.

Clinical Pearl
The fundamental question is: Why is DKA mortality so much lower than HHS? Two reasons: (1) DKA presents early because the GI symptoms are impossible to ignore, so patients get to the hospital sooner. (2) HHS patients are typically older with more comorbidities, and the insidious onset means they present with profound dehydration that is harder to reverse. The takeaway: speed of recognition determines survival in both conditions.

3. Long-Term Complications of Diabetes SLIDES

Chronic hyperglycemia damages blood vessels through two main pathways: macrovascular disease (accelerated atherosclerosis) and microvascular disease (damage to the smallest vessels). The longer diabetes is poorly controlled, the more severe these complications become. This is why the ADA A1C target of < 7% exists—it is the threshold below which complication risk drops significantly.

Macrovascular Complications

Accelerated atherosclerosis affects the coronary arteries (CAD, MI), cerebral vessels (stroke), and peripheral arteries (peripheral vascular disease, claudication). Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in diabetic patients. Risk reduction centers on tight glycemic control, blood pressure management, lipid management, smoking cessation, and antiplatelet therapy.

Microvascular Complications

Diabetic Retinopathy

Retinal damage progresses through three stages: nonproliferative (background), preproliferative, and proliferative. Microaneurysms and hemorrhages in the retina lead to progressive vision loss. Annual dilated eye exams are essential for early detection. Laser photocoagulation can slow progression.

Diabetic Nephropathy

The earliest detectable sign is microalbuminuria (> 30 mg/24h on two consecutive tests). ACE inhibitors are first-line treatment because they slow progression independent of blood pressure effects. Uncontrolled nephropathy progresses to ESKD—creating the direct link between diabetes and the kidney failure discussed in the second half of this document.

Neuropathic Complications

Peripheral Neuropathy

The progression is characteristic: paresthesias and burning sensations → numbness → unsteady gait → Charcot joints (structural destruction from repeated undetected trauma). Monofilament testing is used to assess for loss of protective sensation. The earliest symptom patients notice is tingling in the feet.

Autonomic Neuropathy

Damage to autonomic nerves produces a constellation of seemingly unrelated symptoms: fixed tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension, gastroparesis (delayed gastric emptying—which makes glucose control unpredictable), neurogenic bladder, erectile dysfunction, and hypoglycemic unawareness.

Diabetic Foot Care SLIDES

The foot ulceration cascade demonstrates why neuropathy is so dangerous: minor injury or skin fissure → not detected because of absent sensation → progresses to infection → may lead to amputation. Patient education is critical:

  • Inspect feet daily (use mirror for soles)
  • Never walk barefoot
  • No heating pads or hot water bottles on feet (cannot feel burns)
  • Wear properly fitting shoes
  • Trim nails straight across
  • Report any cuts, blisters, or color changes immediately

4. Kidney Disease Foundations HEAVY IN SLIDES

The kidneys are the body's master regulators of fluid, electrolytes, acid-base balance, blood pressure, and red blood cell production. When they fail, every body system is affected. The two most common causes of kidney failure in the United States are diabetes mellitus and hypertension—directly linking the first half of this document to the second.

The critical distinction to internalize: AKI is reversible (if caught early and the cause is addressed). CKD/ESKD is progressive and irreversible.

4.1 Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) SLIDES

AKI is a sudden, potentially reversible decrease in kidney function characterized by rising BUN and creatinine and, usually, oliguria (urine output < 400 mL/day). The RIFLE classification grades severity: Risk → Injury → Failure → Loss → ESKD.

Three Categories of AKI

Clinical Pearl
Practice questions test whether you understand the mechanism, not just the label. Burns cause prerenal AKI because massive fluid loss creates hypoperfusion. Aminoglycosides cause intrarenal AKI because they are directly toxic to tubular cells. Understanding the "why" helps you predict, prevent, and prioritize.

Four Phases of AKI

Safety Alert — The Diuresis Trap
The diuresis phase is counterintuitive and heavily tested on exams. The kidneys are recovering and beginning to produce urine again—but they cannot yet concentrate it properly. Output can reach 4.5 liters per day. The danger is now dehydration and electrolyte loss, the exact opposite of the oliguric phase. Patients need aggressive fluid and electrolyte replacement during diuresis. Watch for hypokalemia and hyponatremia.

AKI Prevention

Many cases of AKI are iatrogenic—caused or worsened by medical treatment—and therefore preventable:

  • Pre-procedure IV hydration (NS) for patients receiving contrast dye
  • Hold nephrotoxic medications before and after contrast
  • Monitor drug levels for aminoglycosides and vancomycin
  • N-acetylcysteine (Mucomyst) may be prescribed prophylactically before contrast
  • Maintain adequate hydration in any patient receiving nephrotoxic drugs
  • Avoid NSAIDs in patients with any degree of renal impairment

4.2 Chronic Kidney Disease & ESKD HEAVY IN SLIDES

CKD is a progressive, irreversible deterioration of kidney function. As nephrons are destroyed, the remaining nephrons hypertrophy to compensate—but eventually, compensation fails and the accumulation of metabolic waste products (azotemia) produces systemic toxicity affecting every organ system.

Memory Anchor — Renal Osteodystrophy Cascade
Phosphorus UP → Calcium DOWN → Vitamin D DOWN → PTH UP → Bones WEAKEN. Failed kidneys can't excrete phosphorus, so it accumulates. High phosphorus binds calcium (dropping serum calcium). The kidneys also can't convert vitamin D to its active form (calcitriol), so calcium absorption from the gut drops further. The parathyroid glands respond by secreting more PTH, which leaches calcium from bones. The result is brittle, fracture-prone bones.

Anemia Management in CKD/ESKD

The kidneys produce erythropoietin (EPO), which stimulates red blood cell production. In kidney failure, EPO production plummets. Treatment includes epoetin alfa or darbepoetin injections, with target hemoglobin of 10–11.5 g/dL (higher targets increase vascular thrombosis risk). IV iron supplementation is usually needed concurrently, along with folic acid. Monitor for hypertension and vascular access thrombosis as side effects of EPO therapy.

ESKD Nursing Assessment SLIDES

  • Fluid status: daily weights, I&O, edema, lung sounds, JVD
  • Nutritional status: weight trends, albumin, protein intake
  • Electrolyte balance: K+, Na+, Ca, Phos, Mg
  • Activity tolerance and fatigue
  • Self-esteem and coping
  • Potential complications: hyperkalemia, pericarditis, infection

4.3 Electrolyte Disturbances in Renal Failure

Hyperkalemia — The Most Dangerous Electrolyte Emergency HEAVY IN SLIDES

Hyperkalemia is the electrolyte disturbance most likely to kill your patient. The kidneys normally excrete 80% of the body's potassium; when they fail, K+ accumulates rapidly. Combined with metabolic acidosis (which shifts K+ out of cells), dietary indiscretion, and certain medications, serum potassium can reach lethal levels quickly.

Calcium gluconate is always the FIRST intervention in hyperkalemic emergency—not because it lowers potassium (it doesn't), but because it stabilizes the myocardium against fatal dysrhythmias. You're buying time for the other treatments to work. Think of it as a cardiac shield.

5. Renal Replacement Therapy HEAVY IN SLIDES

When the kidneys can no longer sustain life, renal replacement therapy takes over. The three modalities—hemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, and CRRT—use different mechanisms but share the same goal: remove toxic waste products, excess fluid, and restore electrolyte balance. None of them cure kidney disease. They are life support.

5.1 Hemodialysis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Indications

Hemodialysis is indicated when conservative management can no longer control the consequences of kidney failure: rising or dangerously high potassium, fluid overload or impending pulmonary edema, increasing metabolic acidosis, uremic symptoms (pericarditis, encephalopathy, neuropathy), and removal of certain medications or toxins.

Principles of Hemodialysis

Blood is pumped from the patient through an artificial kidney (dialyzer) and back to the patient. Three physical principles drive the process:

  • Diffusion: Solutes (urea, creatinine, electrolytes, drugs) move from high concentration (blood) to low concentration (dialysate) across the semipermeable membrane
  • Osmosis: Water moves from low to high solute concentration
  • Ultrafiltration: Hydrostatic pressure pushes excess water across the membrane. Controlled by adjusting transmembrane pressure

The dialyzer uses a hollow-fiber design: blood flows through thousands of thin capillary fibers while dialysate flows around them in the opposite direction (countercurrent flow), maximizing the concentration gradient and efficiency. A typical HD schedule is three times per week, 3–4 hours per session.

5.2 Peritoneal Dialysis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Peritoneal dialysis uses the patient's own peritoneal membrane (surface area approximately 22,000 cm²) as a natural dialysis membrane. Sterile dialysate is infused through a surgically placed catheter into the peritoneal cavity, dwells for a prescribed time while diffusion and osmosis occur, and then drains by gravity.

Types of Peritoneal Dialysis

TypeProcessLifestyle Impact
CAPD (Continuous Ambulatory)4–5 manual exchanges per day. Infuse (10 min) → Dwell (4–8 hours) → Drain (20–30 min)Patient-independent. No machine. Can do ADLs during dwell
CCPD (Continuous Cyclic)Automated cycler performs 3–5 exchanges overnight while patient sleeps. May have long daytime dwellFrees daytime hours. Machine-dependent at night
Acute IntermittentHospital-based, manual exchanges for acute situationsTemporary

Dialysate comes in different dextrose concentrations: 1.5%, 2.5%, and 4.25%. Higher concentrations pull more fluid across the membrane (greater ultrafiltration) but, used chronically, damage the peritoneal membrane over time. The physician orders the type of dialysate, number of exchanges per day, and dwell/drain times.

Infection Control During PD SLIDES

  • Strict hand hygiene before and during every exchange
  • Wear mask and hair bonnet during exchanges
  • No visitors during exchange; door closed
  • Sterile cap placed on catheter during dwell periods
  • Catheter exit site care: daily or 3–4 times weekly with soap and water during bathing. Do NOT submerge exit site in bathwater

Peritonitis — The Critical Complication HEAVY IN SLIDES

Peritonitis is the most common and most serious complication of peritoneal dialysis. It is caused by contamination during catheter connections/disconnections, exit site infection spreading internally, or translocation of bowel organisms.

Safety Alert — Recognizing Peritonitis
The FIRST sign of peritonitis is CLOUDY DIALYSATE EFFLUENT. Normal effluent is clear and pale yellow. Cloudy effluent = peritonitis until proven otherwise. Other signs include diffuse abdominal pain with rebound tenderness, fever, nausea, general malaise, and in severe cases, hypotension and shock. Obtain a culture of the effluent FIRST, then start antibiotics. Treatment: intraperitoneal (IP) antibiotics added to dialysate plus systemic antibiotics. Continue dialysis during treatment.

Other PD Complications

  • Catheter exit site and tunnel infections
  • Protein loss in dialysate → hypoalbuminemia (malnutrition indicator)
  • Hernias (from increased intra-abdominal pressure)
  • Lower back pain, constipation
  • Leakage at catheter site
  • Bleeding
Clinical Pearl
If drainage is insufficient during PD, the first nursing action is to reposition the patient—roll them to the other side. Constipation is a common cause of poor drainage (bowel pressing on catheter), so maintaining regular bowel function is part of PD care that students often overlook.

5.3 Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT) SLIDES

CRRT is used in the ICU for hemodynamically unstable patients who cannot tolerate the rapid fluid and solute shifts of conventional hemodialysis. Instead of 3–4 hours, CRRT runs continuously over 24+ hours, removing fluid and toxins slowly and gently.

5.4 Nursing the Hospitalized Dialysis Patient HEAVY IN SLIDES

When a patient who receives chronic dialysis is hospitalized for any reason, several critical nursing considerations apply on top of whatever brought them in.

Protecting Vascular Access

Assess the AV fistula or graft at every shift for thrill and bruit. Never use the access arm for BP, blood draws, or IV therapy. If the patient has an HD catheter, only a dialysis nurse or physician should access it. If used in an emergency, withdraw the catheter dwell volume before administering any medication or fluid.

IV Therapy Precautions

Patients on dialysis cannot excrete water. Any IV fluid must be given as slowly as possible to prevent volume overload and pulmonary edema. Use IV pumps for accuracy. Maintain meticulous I&O records.

Cardiovascular Monitoring — Pericarditis to Tamponade SLIDES

Uremic toxin accumulation can damage the pericardium, triggering a dangerous progression:

Memory Anchor
Friction rub = pericarditis. Friction rub DISAPPEARS = effusion (fluid has filled the space and muffled the rubbing). Muffled heart sounds + hypotension + JVD = tamponade = EMERGENCY. The key clinical transition to watch for: a pericarditis patient whose friction rub suddenly goes away is getting worse, not better.

Blood Pressure Management

Antihypertensive medications are typically held before dialysis and given after dialysis. The rationale: dialysis itself removes fluid, lowering blood pressure. If you combine fluid removal with a full dose of antihypertensive, the patient develops dangerous hypotension.

Medication Considerations

Many medications are cleared through dialysis, requiring dose adjustments or timing around treatments. All IV solutions and medications must be evaluated for their potassium and magnesium content. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs. Dialysis patients have increased infection risk (decreased phagocytic ability, low WBC function, impaired platelets) and increased bleeding risk.

Psychological Support

Chronic dialysis patients may consider discontinuing treatment. This must be taken seriously—not dismissed or treated as a crisis. The appropriate response is a psychiatric evaluation to rule out treatable depression, followed by counseling support. If depression is ruled out, the patient's informed decision should be respected as an advance directive.

6. Kidney Surgery & Transplantation SLIDES

6.1 Kidney Surgery

Kidney surgery may be performed to remove obstructions (tumors, calculi), insert drainage tubes, or remove a diseased kidney. Four surgical approaches are used: flank, lumbar, thoracoabdominal, and laparoscopic (least invasive, fastest recovery).

Postoperative Priorities

The kidney is a highly vascular organ. Hemorrhage and shock are the chief complications of kidney surgery. Fluid and blood component replacement is frequently needed in the immediate postoperative period.

6.2 Kidney Transplantation SLIDES

Kidney transplantation is the treatment of choice for selected patients with ESKD, but it is not a cure—the transplant will not function for the entire lifespan of most recipients. It is an elective procedure, not an emergency, and patients should be in the best possible condition beforehand.

Donor Types

Living donor transplants (related or unrelated) have better long-term outcomes than deceased donor transplants. Paired exchange programs allow incompatible donor-recipient pairs to swap with other pairs to find compatible matches. Deceased donor kidneys may experience acute tubular necrosis and take 2–3 weeks to function. Living donor kidneys typically function immediately.

Surgical Placement

The transplanted kidney is placed in the iliac fossa (anterior to the iliac crest) for easier vascular access. The renal artery is sutured to the iliac artery, the renal vein to the iliac vein, and the ureter to the bladder. The patient's native kidneys are usually not removed (except for enlarged polycystic kidneys that risk rupture or infection).

Contraindications to Transplant

  • Recent malignancy
  • Active or chronic infection (HIV, Hepatitis B/C)
  • Severe irreversible extrarenal disease (inoperable cardiac disease, chronic lung disease, severe PVD)
  • BMI > 35 (Class II obesity)
  • Active substance use disorder
  • Inability to give informed consent
  • Active psychiatric disease
  • History of nonadherence to treatment regimens

Safety Alert — Grapefruit Interaction
Grapefruit and grapefruit juice interact with many immunosuppressant medications (particularly cyclosporine and tacrolimus) by inhibiting CYP3A4 enzyme activity, which raises drug levels to potentially toxic ranges. Transplant patients must be educated to avoid all grapefruit products for life.

Long-Term Immunosuppression Risks

The medications themselves carry significant morbidity: nephrotoxicity (ironic but real—the drugs protecting the transplant can damage it), hypertension, hyperlipidemia, hirsutism, tremors, blood dyscrasias, cataracts, gingival hyperplasia, and increased cancer risk. Cardiovascular disease is the #1 cause of morbidity and mortality after transplantation. Patients require lifelong cancer screening beyond standard recommendations.

6.4 Post-Transplant Nursing Management SLIDES

Assessing for Rejection

The nurse monitors for: oliguria, edema, fever, increasing blood pressure, weight gain, and swelling or tenderness over the transplanted kidney. In patients receiving cyclosporine, the only sign of rejection may be an asymptomatic rise in serum creatinine—the classic symptoms may be masked. This is why lab monitoring is non-negotiable.

Preventing Infection

Immunosuppression makes the patient vulnerable to every infection:

  • Monitor WBC and platelet counts closely—immunosuppression depresses both
  • Frequent urine cultures (high incidence of bacteriuria early and late post-transplant)
  • Culture all wound drainage and catheter/drain tips on removal
  • No fresh flowers in the transplant unit
  • Strict hand hygiene by all who contact the patient
  • Most infections occur in the first month (surgical complications, invasive devices, genitourinary focus)
  • After 6 months with stable function: infectious risk decreases if no rejection episodes

Monitoring Urinary Function

Output from the urinary catheter is measured every hour. IV fluids are given based on urine volume and serum electrolyte levels. If the kidney is from a deceased donor, ATN may cause anuria/oliguria/polyuria for 2–3 weeks, during which hemodialysis may be needed to maintain homeostasis.

GI Complications

Corticosteroid-induced GI ulceration and bleeding are managed prophylactically with H2 blockers (famotidine) or PPIs (omeprazole). Fungal colonization of the mouth and urinary bladder may occur from combined immunosuppression and antibiotic therapy—prophylactic oral antifungal rinses are standard.

Patient Education for Discharge

  • Lifelong immunosuppression—never stop medications without provider guidance
  • Report immediately: decreased urine output, weight gain, malaise, fever, respiratory distress, tenderness over graft, changes in eating/drinking habits, blood pressure changes, anxiety, depression
  • Inform ALL health care providers (including dentists) about the transplant and immunosuppressive regimen
  • Avoid contact sports (risk of graft injury)
  • Follow-up: initially 1–2 times per week, tapering over time. Lifelong lab monitoring
  • Cancer screening: higher risk than general population due to immunosuppression
  • Cardiovascular risk management: #1 cause of post-transplant morbidity and mortality
Clinical Pearl
The living organ donor often experiences more pain than the transplant recipient and requires more analgesia. This is a common knowledge gap. Both donor and recipient need the same level of nursing care, including fluid/electrolyte/hemodynamic monitoring. The donor should have routine long-term follow-up for blood pressure monitoring, as one kidney is now handling the workload of two.

Connecting It All: The Diabetes-to-Dialysis Continuum

The material in this document is not two separate topics glued together. It is one continuous pathophysiologic story:

Diabetes is the #1 cause of ESKD. Hypertension is #2. Both are modifiable. The earliest detectable sign of diabetic nephropathy is microalbuminuria, and ACE inhibitors can slow progression at this stage. Every nursing intervention aimed at glycemic control, blood pressure management, and medication adherence is simultaneously protecting the kidneys. By the time a patient reaches dialysis or transplant, the opportunities for prevention are gone—but the nursing expertise required only intensifies.

The Big Picture
When you're teaching a newly diagnosed diabetic patient about A1C goals and foot care, you're not just managing diabetes. You're potentially preventing a future life on dialysis. When you're protecting an AV fistula from a well-meaning phlebotomist, you're protecting that patient's literal lifeline. Every piece of this document connects to every other piece. The better you understand those connections, the better you'll think clinically—and the better your patients will do.
