I. Foundations of Respiratory Function

Gas Exchange & Oxygen Transport

The entire purpose of the respiratory system can be stated in one sentence: deliver oxygen to every cell in the body and remove the carbon dioxide those cells produce. SLIDES Oxygen diffuses from capillary blood, across the capillary wall, and into the interstitial fluid surrounding cells. Carbon dioxide travels the opposite direction—from the cell into the blood—and is transported back to the lungs for exhalation.

Two terms anchor everything that follows. Respiration is the entire process of gas exchange between atmospheric air and blood, and between blood and cells. Ventilation is the mechanical component—the movement of the thoracic walls and diaphragm that physically moves air in and out of the lungs. You can have ventilation without adequate respiration (think dead-space ventilation), and that distinction matters clinically.

The Respiratory Center

Breathing is controlled by the brainstem. The medulla oblongata houses the inspiratory and expiratory centers that set the baseline rate and depth of ventilation. The pons fine-tunes this: the lower pons (apneustic center) promotes deep, prolonged inspiration, while the upper pons (pneumotaxic center) controls the overall pattern of respirations. SLIDES

Two types of chemoreceptors keep this system responsive to metabolic demands:

  • Central chemoreceptors (medulla): respond to pH changes in the cerebrospinal fluid, which reflect blood CO2 levels. When pH drops (more acidic = more CO2), these receptors signal the lungs to increase depth, then rate, of ventilation.
  • Peripheral chemoreceptors (aortic arch and carotid bodies): respond first to changes in PaO2, then to PaCO2 and pH. These are your backup system—they kick in when oxygen levels drop.
Pearl: Why This Matters for COPD

In healthy people, the primary drive to breathe is rising CO2 detected by central chemoreceptors. In chronic CO2 retainers (severe COPD), the central chemoreceptors become desensitized to chronically elevated CO2. Their remaining drive to breathe is hypoxic drive—low O2 detected by peripheral chemoreceptors. This is why high-flow O2 can suppress respiratory drive in COPD patients.

Additional control mechanisms from the textbook: TEXTBOOK

  • Hering-Breuer reflex: stretch receptors in the alveoli inhibit inspiration when the lungs become overdistended—a built-in safety valve against overinflation.
  • Proprioceptors: respond to body movements and increase ventilation. This is the physiologic rationale for range-of-motion exercises and early mobilization in immobile patients.

Pressure, Airflow, and Resistance

Air moves from areas of high pressure to low pressure—that is the entire mechanical basis of breathing. SLIDES During inspiration, the diaphragm contracts and the thoracic cavity expands, creating lower pressure inside the lungs than outside—air flows in. During expiration, the elastic recoil of lung tissue raises alveolar pressure above atmospheric pressure—air flows out.

Airflow resistance is determined by airway diameter and lung volumes. If bronchial diameter changes for any reason, resistance changes with it. Increased resistance means the patient must work harder to move air. SLIDES

Compliance & Resistance

Compliance is the ease with which the lungs can expand under pressure—the elasticity and expandability of the lungs and thoracic structures. Think of it as how stretchy the lung is. HEAVY IN SLIDES

Increased Compliance

The lungs have lost their elastic recoil and are overdistended. Air goes in easily but won't come back out. The classic example is emphysema—the destroyed alveolar walls can no longer spring back during exhalation.

Decreased Compliance

The lungs are stiff and require greater energy to inflate. Conditions that decrease compliance:

  • Pneumothorax, hemothorax
  • Morbid obesity
  • Pleural effusion
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Atelectasis
  • Pulmonary fibrosis
  • ARDS

Memory: Obstructive vs. Restrictive Pattern

Obstructive = air gets trapped (can't get out) → RV, FRC, TLC all increase. Think emphysema: lungs full of trapped air.

Gas Exchange & Partial Pressure

Atmospheric air at sea level exerts 760 mmHg of total pressure. That pressure is shared among the gases present: nitrogen (78%), oxygen (21%), and CO2 (0.04%). The partial pressure of each gas is the fraction of total pressure it contributes. SLIDES

The Oxyhemoglobin Dissociation Curve TEXTBOOK

This sigmoid-shaped curve describes the relationship between PaO2 and how much oxygen hemoglobin actually carries (SaO2). The shape matters enormously at the bedside:

At the top of the curve (PaO2 >70 mmHg), the curve is flat. A patient's PaO2 could drop from 100 to 80 mmHg and their saturation barely changes (97% → ~94%). This is the "safe zone"—there is a cushion.

At the steep part of the curve (PaO2 <60 mmHg), small drops in PaO2 cause dramatic drops in saturation. A PaO2 of 60 gives you ~90% saturation. Drop to 40 and you are at roughly 75%. This is why SpO2 of 90% is the critical threshold—below it, you are on the steep slope and deterioration accelerates.

Pearl: Shift Right = Release

Right shift (decreased Hgb affinity → MORE O2 released to tissues): increased CO2, acidosis, increased temperature, increased 2,3-DPG. These are all states where tissues are metabolically active and need more O2.

Left shift (increased Hgb affinity → LESS O2 released): decreased CO2, alkalosis, decreased temperature, decreased 2,3-DPG. Hemoglobin holds on tighter—tissues may become hypoxic even with adequate SaO2.

Alveolar Anatomy & Surfactant TEXTBOOK

The lungs contain approximately 300 million alveoli with a total surface area of 50-100 m2. Two cell types line them:

  • Type I alveolar cells: 95% of the alveolar surface area. They form the barrier between air and the alveolar surface—the membrane gas must diffuse across.
  • Type II alveolar cells: only 5% of surface area but critically important. They produce surfactant (reduces surface tension, prevents alveolar collapse) and can regenerate Type I cells after injury.

Alveolar macrophages are the resident phagocytes—they ingest foreign matter and bacteria as a key defense mechanism. The conducting airways contain ~150 mL of air that never reaches alveoli, known as anatomic dead space.

Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) Ratios

Adequate gas exchange requires that ventilation (airflow to alveoli) matches perfusion (blood flow to those alveoli). The V/Q ratio describes this balance, and imbalances are the underlying mechanism of hypoxemia in most respiratory diseases. HEAVY IN SLIDES

Warning: Shunt Threshold

Severe hypoxia occurs when shunting exceeds 20%. Shunting is the most common cause of hypoxia after thoracic or abdominal surgery and in most forms of respiratory failure. Supplemental O2 may or may not correct hypoxemia depending on the type of V/Q imbalance—true shunts are often refractory to O2 because the blood simply never encounters ventilated alveoli.

Pulmonary Circulation Details TEXTBOOK

The pulmonary circulation is a low-pressure system (systolic 20-30 mmHg, diastolic 5-15 mmHg)—far lower than systemic circulation. About 2% of blood pumped by the right ventricle never perfuses the alveolar capillaries (physiologic shunt). In an upright person, gravity creates three zones: upper lung (poor blood supply), middle (intermediate), and lower (maximal blood supply). When lying on one side, more blood flows to the dependent (lower) lung.

CO2 Transport TEXTBOOK

Ninety percent of CO2 is carried by red blood cells, 5% is dissolved in plasma (measured as PaCO2), and 5% is carried in other forms. Only 6% of venous CO2 is removed in the lungs with each pass. Normal arterial PaCO2 is approximately 40 mmHg.

II. Respiratory Assessment

Key Symptoms

Dyspnea SLIDES

Dyspnea is the subjective feeling of discomfort when breathing—it is what the patient reports, not what you measure. It is common in cardiac disorders, allergies, anemia, end-of-life states, exercise, PE, and MI. Importantly, dyspnea is a predictor of adverse outcomes. Always assess the degree (sudden vs. gradual onset), associated anxiety, and what makes it better or worse.

Orthopnea is shortness of breath when lying flat, seen in heart disease and COPD. It is relieved by sitting or standing and is measured by the number of pillows needed (2-pillow orthopnea, 3-pillow orthopnea). SLIDES

Cough HEAVY IN SLIDES

Cough is a protective reflex that clears the airways. It can be impaired by weakness, paralysis, immobility, NGT placement, depressed function (sedation, anesthesia, brain injury), or mechanical ventilation. Common causes include asthma, smoke exposure, GERD, ACE inhibitors, and infection.

Stridor HEAVY IN SLIDES

Stridor is a continuous, high-pitched sound heard over the neck, indicating narrowing of the upper respiratory tract. It demands immediate intervention—this is an airway emergency. Watch for it post-extubation.

Physical Assessment Findings

Clubbing of Fingers SLIDES

Finger clubbing is a sign of chronic hypoxic conditions. The nail bed becomes spongy and the angle between the nail and the nail bed flattens, then reverses. Associated conditions: congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease, lung malignancy, endocarditis, and inflammatory bowel disease.

Cyanosis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Cyanosis (bluish skin discoloration) is a late indicator of hypoxia and is not reliable. It appears only when there is at least 5 g/dL of unoxygenated hemoglobin. TEXTBOOK Consider: a patient with Hgb 15 g/dL won't show cyanosis until a full third of their hemoglobin is deoxygenated. An anemic patient may never show cyanosis because they lack enough total hemoglobin to have 5 g/dL become unoxygenated. Conversely, a polycythemic patient may appear cyanotic even when adequately oxygenated.

  • Central cyanosis: assessed on tongue and lips—indicates decreased O2 tension in blood (more reliable)
  • Peripheral cyanosis: fingers, toes, earlobes—may reflect vasoconstriction from cold, not necessarily a systemic problem

Tracheal Assessment SLIDES

The trachea is normally midline. Place your thumb and index finger on either side to assess position. Pneumothorax or pleural effusion may displace the trachea.

Memory: Which Way Does the Trachea Shift?

TOWARD the problem in atelectasis/collapse (the collapsed side pulls things toward it).
AWAY FROM the problem in effusion, pneumothorax, tension pneumothorax (the fluid or air pushes things to the other side).

Thoracic Inspection SLIDES

Assess color, turgor, subcutaneous tissue, and chest configuration. Barrel chest (increased anteroposterior diameter from chronic lung overinflation) is a hallmark of emphysema and COPD. HEAVY IN SLIDES Observe for use of accessory muscles: sternocleidomastoid, scalene, and trapezius during inspiration; abdominal and intercostal muscles during expiration. Also assess for nasal flaring.

Tactile Fremitus SLIDES

Tactile fremitus detects chest wall vibrations when the patient says "99" or "one, one, one." Use the palmar surface of your hands. Fremitus is more pronounced in men (deeper voice) and increased on the right side (large bronchi are closer to the chest wall). It is decreased or absent over the anterior chest (overlies the heart).

The key clinical principle: air does not conduct sound well.

  • Emphysema: almost no tactile fremitus (hyperinflated, air-filled lungs)
  • Pneumonia: increased fremitus over the affected lobe (consolidated, fluid-filled tissue conducts vibration better)

III. Diagnostics & ABG Interpretation

ETCO2 & Capnography HEAVY IN SLIDES

End-tidal CO2 (ETCO2) monitoring deserves special attention because the lecture emphasized it repeatedly as a more accurate predictor of respiratory status than SpO2. Here is why: SpO2 measures oxygenation (how much O2 hemoglobin carries), but ETCO2 measures ventilation (how effectively the patient is moving air and exchanging CO2). ETCO2 provides a breath-by-breath reflection of ventilation status and assesses airway, breathing, AND circulation simultaneously.

Normal ETCO2 range is 35-45 mmHg (same as PaCO2). The colorimetric detector changes from purple to yellow when CO2 is detected. Critically, ETCO2 identifies respiratory depression and airway complications before clinical observation—before you see the patient's SpO2 drop, before you notice the change in breathing pattern.

Pearl: The ETCO2 Paradox in Sedation

Sedation may induce respiratory depression, causing shallow breathing. Gas in the alveoli is not completely exhaled and becomes diluted by gas in the trachea and large airways (which has no CO2). So blood and alveolar CO2 actually rise, but expired CO2 appears to decrease. This is why waveform monitoring matters—look at the pattern, not just the number.

IV. Oxygen Therapy & Delivery

Oxygen is a medication—use the lowest amount necessary. HEAVY IN SLIDES

Hypoxemia & Hypoxia

Hypoxemia is decreased arterial O2 (PaO2 <60 mmHg). It manifests as agitation, confusion, lethargy, increased BP, diaphoresis, heart rate changes, and central cyanosis (a LATE sign). Hypoxemia leads to hypoxia—decreased O2 delivery to tissues. Rapid O2 deterioration causes CNS changes early. In chronic O2 deprivation (COPD with hypercarbia), patients present with delayed reactions, lethargy, and drowsiness. SLIDES

Warning: O2 Toxicity & COPD

O2 toxicity leads to lung damage similar to ARDS—avoid prolonged high FiO2 when possible.

In COPD patients: high-flow O2 depresses respiratory drive. Alveolar hypoventilation cannot accommodate increased O2. Maintain SpO2 >90% at the lowest effective flow rate.

V. Upper Respiratory Tract Disorders

Viral Rhinitis (Common Cold) SLIDES

Up to 200 different viruses cause the common cold. Presentation: low-grade temperature, nasal congestion, rhinorrhea, sneezing, sore throat, muscle aches. Treatment is supportive: fluids, rest, NSAIDs, antihistamines, expectorants. The virus is shed for 2 days before symptoms appear, making it highly contagious.

Rhinitis SLIDES

Inflammation and irritation of the nasal mucous membranes. Allergic rhinitis is triggered by dust, dander, pollen, and mold. Drug-induced rhinitis can be caused by ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, statins, antidepressants, antianxiety medications, and aspirin. Treatment: antihistamines and corticosteroid nasal sprays.

Rhinitis medicamentosa is rebound nasal congestion from overuse of OTC topical decongestants (phenylephrine, oxymetazoline). TEXTBOOK Key teaching: topical decongestants should NOT be used for more than 3-4 days.

Rhinosinusitis SLIDES

Inflammation of the paranasal sinuses and nasal cavity, typically following a cold. Bacterial or viral. Presents with facial pressure, pain, colored nasal discharge, and high fever.

Rare but serious: osteomyelitis, orbital cellulitis (→ ptosis, proptosis, chemosis → loss of vision if pressure on optic nerve), cavernous sinus thrombosis, meningitis, brain abscess.

Pharyngitis SLIDES

Inflammation of the pharynx. Most cases are viral; 15% are Group A Streptococcus (GAS)—these need antibiotics. Presentation: pain, fever, vasodilation, edema, redness, possible creamy exudate on tonsils, malaise, bad breath.

GAS pharyngitis: fiery-red pharyngeal membrane, swollen tonsils with white-purple exudate, enlarged tender cervical lymph nodes, NO cough, fever >38.3°C. Treatment: Penicillin V potassium × 10 days (first-line). TEXTBOOK

Warning: Untreated Strep Pharyngitis

Complications include rheumatic fever (onset 2-3 weeks post-pharyngitis) and glomerulonephritis. Full course of antibiotics is critical—not just symptom resolution.

Tonsillitis & Peritonsillar Abscess SLIDES

Tonsillitis: less common than pharyngitis; most common pathogen is Epstein-Barr virus. If bacterial, penicillin is first-line. Chronic cases may require tonsillectomy.

Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy): most common major suppurative complication of sore throat. Adults 20-40. Purulent exudate after tonsillar infection that can spread to neck and chest. Key findings: severe sore throat, fever, trismus (inability to open mouth), drooling, intense pain, rancid breath, raspy voice. Treatment: antimicrobials + steroids; may need needle aspiration/I&D. Can be life-threatening if it spreads (mediastinitis, airway obstruction).

Laryngitis SLIDES

Inflammation of the larynx. Hoarseness, aphonia (loss of voice), severe cough. Treatment: voice rest, avoid irritants, inhalation of cool steam.

Epistaxis TEXTBOOK

Most common site: anterior septum (Kiesselbach plexus). Initial management: sit upright, tilt head FORWARD (not back), pinch soft nose against septum for 5-10 minutes. If persistent: silver nitrate cautery, nasal packing, or balloon catheter. Risk factors: local infection, HTN, anticoagulants/aspirin, thrombocytopenia, nasal trauma, liver disease.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) HEAVY IN SLIDES

Recurrent upper airway obstruction during sleep with reduced ventilation. Risk factors: obesity (major—increased neck circumference, peripharyngeal fat), large neck, male sex, post-menopause, older age. Prevalence is ~26% of adults aged 30-70, and up to 90% are undiagnosed. SLIDES

Warning: OSA Cardiovascular Risk

High prevalence of HTN; high risk for MI and stroke. All hypertensive adults should be screened for OSA. Episodes of hypoxia and hypercapnia trigger sympathetic activation, driving up blood pressure.

Diagnostic criteria: episodes of apnea during sleep for ≥10 seconds, ≥5 episodes/hour, awakening with a snort, loud snoring. Diagnosed with polysomnography (overnight sleep study monitoring EEG, ECG, EMG, airflow, pulse oximetry, leg movements).

Assessment findings: excessive daytime sleepiness, frequent nocturnal awakening, insomnia, morning headaches, personality changes, impotence, HTN, arrhythmias, pulmonary HTN, cor pulmonale, polycythemia, enuresis.

Treatment ladder:

  1. Weight loss, avoid alcohol, positional therapy (avoid sleeping on back)
  2. MAD (mandibular advancement device)—effective for mild-moderate
  3. CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure)—prevents airway collapse
  4. BiPAP—for severe cases with hypoxemia/hypercapnia
  5. Surgery: tonsillectomy, UPPP (50% effective), septoplasty

Laryngeal Cancer HEAVY IN SLIDES

One-quarter of all head and neck cancers; most common in patients >65 years. Almost all are squamous cell carcinoma. 55% present with lymph node involvement at diagnosis. Recurrence usually within the first 2-3 years. SLIDES

Risk factors: tobacco, asbestos, paint fumes, wood dust, chemicals, nutritional deficiencies, alcohol abuse, male gender, African Americans.

Manifestations by location TEXTBOOK:

  • Glottic: hoarseness >2 weeks (early sign—tumor impedes vocal cords)
  • Supraglottic/subglottic: voice changes NOT always early; may present with persistent cough, sore throat, dysphagia, lump in neck
  • Late: dysphagia, dyspnea, weight loss, ear pain (referred)

Treatment goals: preserve swallowing, speech, and breathing without tracheostomy when possible. Options: surgery (vocal cord stripping, cordectomy, laser excision, partial or total laryngectomy), radiation, chemotherapy.

Laryngectomy HEAVY IN SLIDES

Partial Laryngectomy

Used for early-stage glottic tumors with one cord involvement. High cure rate. Airway remains intact. No expected swallowing difficulty. Voice may be hoarse but preserved.

Total Laryngectomy

Complete removal of larynx (hyoid, epiglottis, cricoid cartilage, 2-3 tracheal rings). Curative in most laryngeal cancers. Results in: permanent vocal loss and permanent tracheostomy.

Communication alternatives: writing, lip reading, esophageal speech, artificial (electric) larynx, tracheoesophageal puncture (TEP) with Blom-Singer valve (most resembles normal speech).

Risk Factors & Special Populations SLIDES

  • DM, HF, alcoholism, COPD (H. influenzae), CF (pseudomonal and staph), influenza (staph PNA), smoking, immobility, depressed cough reflex
  • AIDS → Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP)—opportunistic infection

  • Administer antibiotics, antipyretics, antitussives, decongestants
  • Fluids (caution in older adults and/or CHF—fluid overload risk)
  • Respiratory treatments, bed rest, position changes, early ambulation
  • O2, deep breathing/coughing, proper nutrition

Complications: shock, respiratory failure, pleural effusion.

Sarcoidosis SLIDES

An interstitial lung disease presenting in ages 20-40, thought to be a hypersensitivity reaction to an exogenous agent with inherited predisposition. The inflammatory process produces noninfectious granulomas that reduce lung compliance, cause fibrosis, and reduce lung volumes.

Manifestations: dyspnea, cough, hemoptysis, congestion, anorexia, fatigue, weight loss, joint pain, fever, granulomatous lesions (skin, liver, kidney, spleen, CNS). Confirmed by biopsy. PFTs may show reduced TLC; ABGs may show hypoxemia and hypercapnia.

Treatment: corticosteroids (taper after 12 months). If unable to reduce steroid dose, add immune modulator (methotrexate).

VII. Obstructive Pulmonary Disease

Atelectasis HEAVY IN SLIDES

Atelectasis is the collapse of alveoli—either acute or chronic. Acute atelectasis is most common in the post-operative setting. It can be obstructive (blockage prevents air from entering alveoli) or nonobstructive (reduced ventilation). Can also occur from increased external pressure: pleural effusion, pneumothorax, hemothorax. SLIDES

Manifestations: SOB, cough, sputum production, tachycardia, tachypnea, pleural pain, central cyanosis, increased WOB, decreased arterial O2, diminished breath sounds, crackles.

Prevention & Nursing Interventions SLIDES

  • Position changes, ambulation, OOB to chair
  • Deep breathing and coughing, incentive spirometry
  • Suction as needed; postural drainage
  • Pain control (multimodal—opioids depress respiratory drive)
Pearl: ICOUGH Protocol

I = Incentive Spirometry | C = Coughing & Deep Breathing | O = Oral Care | U = Understanding (patient education) | G = Get OOB to chair ×3 daily | H = HOB >30°

Incentive Spirometry SLIDES

Expands alveoli, reduces or treats atelectasis. Patient should be in semi-Fowler or upright position. Use diaphragmatic breathing. Cough during and after each session. Splint the incision when coughing postoperatively. Perform approximately 10 breaths per session, repeat each waking hour.

Chest Physiotherapy / Postural Drainage SLIDES

Secretions drain from bronchioles into bronchi/trachea, then are coughed or suctioned out. Secretions pool in the lung bases. Bronchodilators or mucolytics improve drainage. Chest percussion (machine or manual) can help loosen secretions. Performed 2-3 times daily PRIOR to meals (reduce N/V and aspiration risk). SLIDES

COPD HEAVY IN SLIDES

COPD is a slowly progressive obstructive disease with reduced airflow and impaired O2/CO2 exchange. Tobacco smoke accounts for 80-90% of cases. Additional risk factors: secondhand smoke, increased age, occupational exposure (dust, chemicals), indoor/outdoor air pollution, and alpha1-antitrypsin deficiency (genetic enzyme inhibitor that normally protects lung tissue from destruction). SLIDES

Emphysema

Destruction of overdistended alveoli from chronic inflammation. Alveolar walls are destroyed → increased dead space → no gas exchange can occur. Chronic cough, sputum production, SOB.

Leads to hypoxemia, hypercapnia, respiratory acidosis, and cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure from long-term high BP in pulmonary arteries → backup in venous system, dependent edema, JVD).

Hallmarks: barrel chest, weight loss, SOB on exertion. Increased in fall/winter when infections rise.

Chronic Bronchitis

Definition: cough and sputum production for 3 months in 2 consecutive years.

Smoke and irritants inflame the airways causing increased mucous hypersecretion, mucous plugging, reduced ciliary function. Bronchial walls thicken, the lumen narrows. Alveoli and bronchioles become fibrosed → increased respiratory infections.

COPD Management SLIDES

Smoking cessation (gum, inhaler, patch, sublingual tablet, lozenge; medications: bupropion SR, nortriptyline, clonidine, Chantix). Not candidates for cessation meds: pregnant patients, adolescents, light smokers (≤10/day). Safety: no smoking with home O2.

Bronchodilators: relieve bronchospasm, improve expiratory flow (for stable COPD). Corticosteroids: improve symptoms but do NOT slow disease progression; inhaled preferred (long-term oral → muscle weakness, respiratory failure). SLIDES

Annual flu vaccine HEAVY IN SLIDES — heavily emphasized in lecture.

O2 therapy: maintain SpO2 >90%. Remember: high O2 depresses respiratory drive in COPD patients.

Asthma HEAVY IN SLIDES

Chronic airway inflammation leading to recurrent episodes of cough, chest tightness, wheezing, and SOB—often early morning or late at night. Allergies are the strongest predisposing factor: seasonal, dust, dander, mold, pollens, roach droppings, air pollution, smoke. SLIDES

During exacerbations, bronchoconstriction occurs, causing increased respiratory effort, diaphoresis, tachycardia, widened pulse pressure, hypoxemia, and central cyanosis.

Medications: SABA (rescue)—albuterol/Ventolin, relax smooth muscle for quick relief. LABA (controller)—salmeterol, used WITH anti-inflammatory to control symptoms. Daily Peak Flow monitoring recommended for moderate-to-severe persistent asthma.

Status Asthmaticus HEAVY IN SLIDES

A severe, rapid, persistent asthma attack that does NOT respond to regular therapy. Labored breathing, wheezing, engorged neck veins, prolonged exhalation.

Warning: Silent Chest in Status Asthmaticus

If wheezing disappears, this may signal impending respiratory failure—it means air movement has decreased so severely there is not enough flow to produce a wheeze. Increasing PaCO2 is a warning sign.

Medical treatment: SABA, systemic corticosteroids, supplemental O2, IVF, Mg Sulfate 2g over 20 minutes (smooth muscle relaxation), ABGs. If respiratory acidosis increasing and patient fatigued → possible mechanical ventilation.

Nursing: actively assess airway and response to treatment; be prepared for next intervention; assess skin turgor for dehydration; cardiac monitoring for arrhythmias; no flowers/smoke/perfumes/chemicals; educate on asthma action plan.

Cystic Fibrosis (CF) SLIDES

Autosomal recessive disease, most common in Caucasians. Most diagnosed at age 2; median survival late 30s. Presents with productive cough, wheezing, hyperinflation of lung fields. Chronic inflammation and infection with impaired mucous clearance. SLIDES

Airway clearance is the KEY to management: chest PT, anti-inflammatory agents. Long-term high-dose NSAIDs (ages 6-17) slow loss of lung function. Nursing: symptom management, secretion removal, chest PT, education on fluid intake and nutrition, end-of-life care planning. Chest PT best performed before meals (avoid vomiting), before bed and in the morning.

VIII. Lung Cancer & Thoracic Surgery

Lung Cancer SLIDES

Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death in the United States. The most common cause is inhaled carcinogens, with cigarette smoke responsible for >85% of cases. Cancer arises from a single epithelial cell that undergoes abnormal growth leading to malignancy. SLIDES

IX. Pleural Disorders & Chest Trauma

Pleurisy SLIDES

The parietal pleura has many sensory nerves stimulated by inflammation and stretching. The visceral pleura lacks sensory nerves. Pleuritic pain is sharp, "catches" on inspiration, described as being "stabbed by a knife." It is typically one-sided and worsened by deep breathing, coughing, and sneezing. Management: lay on the affected side to splint the chest wall; splint with a pillow when coughing. May develop with pneumonia, URI, TB, or PE.

Pleural Effusion SLIDES

Fluid collection in the pleural space (between parietal and visceral pleurae). Normal pleural fluid: 5-15 mL. Accumulates as a complication of HF, TB, pneumonia, PE, viral infections. Can be clear, bloody, or purulent. SLIDES

  • Pneumonia-related effusion: fever, chills, pleuritic pain
  • Malignancy-related effusion: dyspnea when flat, coughing; fluid reaccumulates within days/weeks

Assessment: diminished/absent breath sounds, decreased fremitus, dull/flat percussion. Diagnosed by CXR, CT, thoracentesis. Treat the cause, prevent reaccumulation, relieve pain and respiratory compromise.

Empyema SLIDES

Thick, purulent fluid collection in the pleural space. Fibrin walls off the infection. Signs similar to pneumonia plus fever, night sweats, pleural pain, cough, dyspnea, weight loss. Treatment: thoracentesis (if fluid not too thick), chest tube or wedge resection, 4-6 weeks of antibiotics.

Blunt Chest Trauma SLIDES

Most common cause: MVA (steering wheel, seat belts, falls, bicycle handlebars). Can cause chest wall fractures, dislocations, injuries to pleura, lungs, heart, and great vessels. Results in hypoxemia, hypovolemia, cardiac failure (tamponade). Assess and treat aggressively: establish advanced airway, restore fluid volume.

Flail Chest HEAVY IN SLIDES

Three or more ribs fractured at two or more sites—this creates a free-floating segment of chest wall that has lost stability and impairs respirations. The hallmark finding is paradoxical movement: the flail segment moves inward on inhalation and outward on exhalation (opposite of normal chest wall movement). This decreases ventilation and gas exchange. Fix with hooks/wires for stabilization. Lung contusion is common; patient may be sensitive to fluid overload. Can be fatal if untreated.

Penetrating Chest Trauma SLIDES

Restore and maintain cardiopulmonary function. Establish airway, assess for shock and additional injuries. High risk of intra-abdominal injuries. Diagnostics: CXR, ABG, continuous pulse oximetry, continuous cardiac monitoring. Treatment: large-bore IV, fluid/blood replacement, type and crossmatch, initiate massive hemorrhage protocol, NGT (decompress), Foley (urine output), chest tube (re-inflate lung).

Pneumothorax & Hemothorax HEAVY IN SLIDES

Pneumothorax: air in the pleural space. Hemothorax: blood in the pleural space.

Warning: Tension Pneumothorax

Air enters the pleural space and is trapped—it creates positive pressure. The affected lung collapses. The heart, great vessels, and trachea deviate to the UNAFFECTED side. Both respiratory and circulatory function are compromised: increased intrathoracic pressure → decreased venous return → decreased cardiac output → impaired circulation. Pulse may become undetectable (PEA—think H's and T's). Treatment: re-expand the lung, get air out of the pleural space.

Subcutaneous Emphysema SLIDES

Air trapped in tissue under the skin (neck, chest, arms, face). Identified by a crackling sensation when palpated (crepitus). Air is spontaneously absorbed in most cases. In severe cases, tracheostomy may be needed if the airway is threatened.

X. Acute Respiratory Failure & ARDS

Acute Respiratory Failure HEAVY IN SLIDES

Acute respiratory failure is defined by three criteria occurring together: PaO2 <60 mmHg AND PaCO2 >50 mmHg (hypercapnia) AND pH <7.35 (acidosis). Differentiate acute from chronic: chronic respiratory failure progresses over time or persists after an acute event (e.g., COPD). Chronic patients can develop acute exacerbations (aCOPD). SLIDES

Causes

  • Drug overdose, head trauma, infection, hemorrhage, OSA
  • Neuromuscular: Guillain-Barré, ALS, spinal cord trauma
  • Musculoskeletal: chest trauma
  • Oxygenation failures: ARDS, HF, COPD, PE
  • Ventilation failures post-surgery: anesthesia, sedation, decreased respiratory drive

Early Signs

  • Restlessness
  • Fatigue, headache
  • Dyspnea, air hunger
  • Tachycardia
  • Accessory muscle use
  • Elevated blood pressure

Late Signs

  • Confusion, lethargy
  • Tachycardia, tachypnea
  • Central cyanosis
  • Diaphoresis

Nursing Care for ARF SLIDES

  • Assist with intubation, administer sedation
  • Insert orogastric tube, assist central line insertion, Foley catheter
  • Apply restraints as needed, continuous cardiac/respiratory monitoring
  • Turning q2, oral care q2 (prevent VAP), SCDs
  • All components of VAP bundle

ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome) HEAVY IN SLIDES

ARDS is a severe inflammatory process causing diffuse alveolar damage and progressive pulmonary edema. The defining feature: hypoxemia that is unresponsive to O2 therapy regardless of increased PEEP. Reduced lung compliance. CXR shows bilateral infiltrates resembling cardiogenic pulmonary edema. Mortality: 27-50%, with MODS and septic shock as the major cause of death. SLIDES

Trigger event → Release of cellular/chemical mediators → Capillary membrane injury → Structural lung damage ↓ Severe V/Q mismatch → Alveolar collapse (loss of surfactant) → Decreased compliance → Severe hypoxemia ↓ Increased dead space → Stiff, non-compliant lungs → Refractory to supplemental O2

Risk Factors SLIDES

  • Aspiration (gastric secretions, drowning), COVID-19 PNA, drug ingestion/overdose
  • Fat or air embolism
  • Hematologic disorders (DIC, massive transfusions, cardiopulmonary bypass)
  • Localized infection (bacterial, fungal, viral PNA)
  • Major surgery, metabolic disorders (pancreatitis, uremia)
  • Prolonged high O2 concentration inhalation, smoke, corrosive substances
  • Sepsis, shock (any cause), trauma (pulmonary contusion, multiple fractures, head injury)

ARDS Phases & Management SLIDES

Acute Phase (severe, rapid onset <72 hours after event): CXR looks like pulmonary edema with bilateral infiltrates. Increased alveolar dead space, stiff non-compliant lungs. Arterial hypoxemia unresponsive to supplemental O2.

Management:

  • Intubate; baseline temperature prior to paralytic
  • Sedate AND paralyze (DO NOT remove sedation if chemically paralyzed)
  • Add nitric oxide to reduce V/Q mismatch
  • Fluid and nutrition support
  • Maintain SpO2 >90%
  • Suction as needed; frequent ABG monitoring
  • Skin care, frequent oral care, eye care (corneal abrasions from chemical paralysis)
  • Aggressive support: high PEEP, fluid support, vasopressors, prone positioning

Recovery Phase: hypoxemia lessens, CXR improves, lungs become more compliant.

Warning: Complications of Chemical Paralysis

VTE, muscle atrophy, skin breakdown, foot drop, hemorrhage. Higher PEEP may help lower FiO2 but can cause hypotension from capillary leakage and decreased cardiac output.

XI. Airway Management & Mechanical Ventilation

Endotracheal Intubation HEAVY IN SLIDES

Oral or nasal tube placed into the trachea (oral is more common and less traumatic). Maintains the airway during respiratory distress. A laryngoscope is used for placement; the tube is positioned 2 cm above the carina. The cuff is inflated to prevent air leak and secure the tube. Placement is confirmed with CXR. SLIDES

ETT should be converted to a tracheostomy at 14-21 days to reduce trauma to the tracheal lining, prevent vocal cord paralysis, and prevent fistula formation.

Extubation: check for cuff leak, stridor, and work of breathing post-removal.

ETT Bedside Safety & Assessment SLIDES

  • Measure ETT markings at the start of every shift
  • Ambu-bag with facemask, suction (Yankauer), O2 flow meter at bedside
  • Vent settings as ordered
  • Assess for vent synchrony (agitation, restlessness, nasal flaring, accessory muscle use)
  • HOB >30 degrees

Tracheostomy SLIDES

Tracheotomy = the procedure (opening into the trachea). Tracheostomy tube = the device placed. Opening between the 2nd and 3rd tracheal rings. Usually done in OR, bronchoscopy suite, or bedside in ICU for emergencies.

Indications: bypass upper airway obstruction, long-term mechanical ventilation, prevent aspiration in paralyzed/unconscious patient, replace ETT, emergent situations.

Warning: Cuff Pressure

High cuff pressure → tracheal bleeding, ischemia, pressure necrosis.
Low cuff pressure → aspiration risk.

Trach bedside safety: extra trach set at bedside, obturator at bedside, Ambu-bag with facemask, suction, Yankauer, O2 flow meter.

Mechanical Ventilation SLIDES

The most invasive form of oxygen treatment. ICU nurse and respiratory therapist assess for vent synchrony. Vent alarms must always be ON. Tidal Volume = volume of each breath.

Suctioning SLIDES

Use inline suction to maintain sterility. Increase O2 to 100% before suctioning. Suction for no longer than 10 seconds.

Ventilator Weaning & Extubation SLIDES

Daily weaning trial when physiologically and hemodynamically stable. Patient must be able to spontaneously breathe and be recovering from the underlying condition. Stable vitals and ABGs are necessary.

Monitor SpO2, CO2, HR, RR, BP during weaning. CPAP mode enables spontaneous breathing with some pressure support. Monitor for reduced TV.

Warning: Signs of Weaning Failure

Rising CO2, increasing HR and RR, diaphoresis, nasal flaring, accessory muscle use. If CO2 rises from 36 to 65, HR increases from 102 to 126, and RR increases from 20 to 35 with diaphoresis—the patient is not tolerating the wean.

Extubation Process SLIDES

  1. Obtain order; notify respiratory therapist
  2. RT checks for cuff leak (ensures minimal stridor risk)
  3. Explain procedure to patient
  4. Raise HOB
  5. Set up suction at bedside
  6. O2 flow meter for aerosolized mask
  7. Suction through ETT and oral cavity
  8. Remove OGT at time of extubation
  9. Monitor for respiratory distress and stridor
  10. Keep NPO until safe for swallow evaluation

Geriatric Considerations with Mechanical Ventilation SLIDES

Older patients are often sedated with benzos and opioids (potentially inappropriate per Beers Criteria), often restrained, immobilized from length of time on vent, and sensory deprived—all leading to delirium. Use minimal sedation, reduce restraints, mobilize daily. Every day in bed leads to significant deconditioning. Use patient's glasses and hearing aids. Once extubated, allow dentures.

Airway Management SLIDES

Airways can become obstructed by food, vomit, blood, or mucous. Airway enlargement concerns: swelling, laryngeal edema, carcinoma, abscess. Patients needing airway protection: ALOC (loss of protective reflexes), seizures, neck/head trauma, anaphylaxis, neuromuscular weakness.

XII. Pulmonary Embolism & Pulmonary Hypertension

Pulmonary Embolism (PE) HEAVY IN SLIDES

Obstruction of the pulmonary artery originating from the venous system or right side of the heart. Can be a blood clot, fat embolus, amniotic fluid, or air embolus. Gas exchange is impaired or absent. DVT is the most common cause (VTE = term encompassing both PE and DVT). SLIDES

Warning: Time-Critical Emergency

Death often occurs within 1 hour of symptom onset. Assess frequently. Do not dismiss sudden-onset chest pain or SOB.

Manifestations: SOB, chest pain (sudden, pleuritic), substernal pain (mimics angina), anxiety, tachycardia, cough, syncope.

PE Treatment SLIDES

PREVENTION is key: SCDs, subQ heparin, Lovenox, ambulation. For active PE: start heparin GTT, discharge home with long-term anticoagulation. Assess vitals q2; monitor INR, aPTT, PTT, anti-Xa. Thrombolytics if no contraindications. Surgical removal if large PE and unstable. IVC filter for patients with recurrent PEs.

Pulmonary Hypertension SLIDES

High pressure in the pulmonary artery causes the heart to work harder and weakens over time. Not evident until late progression. Risk factors: congenital heart disease, appetite suppressants, stimulant medications, portal HTN, HIV.

Dyspnea is the main symptom—first with exertion, then at rest. Other findings: substernal chest pain, weakness, fatigue, syncope, right-sided HF, distended neck veins, anorexia, RUQ pain.

Diagnosed: history, CXR, PFT, ECG, echocardiogram. Management: calcium channel blockers, prostanoids, phosphodiesterase inhibitors, diuretics, O2, anticoagulation, digoxin, exercise.

Nursing: identify high-risk patients (COPD, PE, congenital heart disease), administer medications, provide emotional support, refer to support groups.

XIII. COVID-19

Pathophysiology & Transmission SLIDES

COVID-19 is caused by SARS-CoV-2, a novel beta coronavirus (like MERS and SARS) that originated in bats. Primary transmission is via respiratory droplets (coughing, sneezing, singing, talking, breathing within 6 feet). Airborne transmission occurs during aerosol-generating procedures (intubation, extubation, suctioning, resuscitation). Individuals may be most infectious early in the disease, often before symptom onset.

Incubation: 2-14 days (average 4-5 days). Classifications:

  • Mild: no or mild pneumonia
  • Severe: dyspnea, hypoxia, respiratory distress
  • Critical: respiratory failure, shock, multisystem organ failure

Risk Factors SLIDES

Advanced age (>80 years = highest mortality), cancer, chronic conditions, obesity, smoking, male sex. African American, Hispanic, and South Asian individuals account for higher hospitalizations and deaths due to socioeconomic disparities. Minorities have lower vaccination rates.

Treatment SLIDES

  • Remdesivir: only FDA-approved drug for hospitalized COVID-19 patients
  • Corticosteroids: limited to patients requiring supplemental O2
  • Prone positioning: improves oxygenation (12-16 hours/day when mechanically ventilated)
  • HFNC: up to 60 L/min in adults (often 100% FiO2 with non-rebreather on top); airborne precautions due to aerosolizing nature
  • ECMO: for refractory respiratory failure (outcomes still evolving)

COVID-19 Nursing Interventions HEAVY IN SLIDES

  • Administer prescribed medications
  • Assess respiratory effort, WOB, accessory muscle use
  • Sedate for vent synchrony; collaborate with RT for FiO2 titration
  • Consider chemical paralytic (monitor TOF)
  • Prone positioning: pad bony prominences, change electrode placement to back
  • Common for pneumothorax due to high PEEP—keep chest tray at bedside (atrium, Vaseline gauze, sterile water, Kelly clamps)
  • OGT, Foley, central line
  • Oral care q2, SCDs or anticoagulation (increased clotting risk)
  • Check blood sugars q6 (tube feedings + steroids increase blood glucose)
  • ECMO may be used for refractory cases; dialysis often needed (kidney failure)
  • Administer vasopressors to support BP

PPE Protocol SLIDES

Standard contact/droplet: gowns, gloves, surgical mask, face shield or goggles. Aerosol-generating procedures: N95 or PAPR. Proper donning and doffing sequence is critical—observed by trained colleague to decrease contamination risk. Donning occurs outside patient area; doffing begins inside patient area.

Effects on Healthcare Workers SLIDES

Burnout, compassion fatigue, depression, PTSD. Early signs: difficulty concentrating, anger, withdrawal, nightmares. Interventions: talk to trusted colleagues, limit news/social media, exercise, meditation, notify supervisor, engage employer mental health services.
